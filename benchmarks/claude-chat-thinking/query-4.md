# Four decades of U.S. regional bank failures

**The same handful of failure patterns — concentrated risk, uninsured deposit flight, regulatory lag, and fraud — have destroyed American banks repeatedly since 1984, yet each generation of bankers and regulators re-learns these lessons only after billions are lost.** From Continental Illinois's pioneering electronic bank run to Silicon Valley Bank's 44-hour collapse via social media, the mechanics of failure have accelerated while the root causes remain stubbornly constant. This report examines 12 notable failures spanning 1984–2023, classifying each by primary failure category, mode of collapse, warning signs, and outcome to reveal the recurring causal patterns that define American banking fragility.

---

## The 1980s: when deregulation met reckless lending

The 1980s produced some of the most consequential bank failures in U.S. history, driven by a toxic combination of financial deregulation, energy-sector concentration, and outright fraud. The era's failures established precedents — including "too big to fail" — that shaped regulatory responses for the next four decades.

### Continental Illinois (1984) — Liquidity/Contagion

Continental Illinois National Bank, then the **7th-largest U.S. bank with $40 billion in assets**, became the first institution to demonstrate that an electronic bank run could destroy a major bank in days. The causal chain began when Continental purchased over **$1 billion in energy loan participations** from Penn Square Bank, a reckless Oklahoma shopping-mall lender. Penn Square's July 1982 failure exposed Continental's concentrated portfolio. After two years of slow deterioration — nonperforming loans reaching **$2.3 billion** by Q1 1984 — rumors of insolvency triggered a massive electronic run starting May 9, 1984. Institutional depositors drained **$10.8 billion in roughly 10 days**, roughly 30% of the bank's funding. Only **15% of Continental's liabilities were insured retail deposits**, leaving the bank catastrophically exposed to wholesale funding flight.

The FDIC responded with an unprecedented bailout: a **$1.5 billion capital infusion**, purchase of **$4.5 billion in bad loans**, and a guarantee covering all depositors and creditors. The government effectively nationalized the bank, acquiring 80% of shares. Congressman Stewart McKinney's description of the rescue popularized the term **"too big to fail."** OCC Chairman C. Todd Conover subsequently confirmed this doctrine would apply to the 11 largest U.S. banks, creating moral hazard that persisted for decades. Continental was re-privatized by 1991 and acquired by Bank of America in 1994. The estimated cost to the FDIC was **$1.1 billion**.

### Lincoln Savings and Loan (1989) — Fraud/Regulatory capture

Lincoln Savings exemplified how fraud and political corruption could neutralize regulatory safeguards. Charles Keating acquired the $1.1 billion Irvine, California thrift in 1984 and **quintupled its assets to $5.5 billion** in four years by pivoting from conservative home lending to speculative commercial real estate, land development, and junk bonds. When the San Francisco Federal Home Loan Bank recommended conservatorship in 1987, **Keating deployed $1.3 million in campaign contributions** to five U.S. senators — the infamous "Keating Five" (Cranston, DeConcini, Glenn, McCain, Riegle) — who pressured regulators to back off. FHLBB Chairman Danny Wall transferred the investigation from San Francisco to Washington, delaying action by nearly two years.

The seizure on April 14, 1989 cost taxpayers approximately **$3.4 billion**. More devastatingly, over **21,000 mostly elderly investors** lost approximately **$285 million** in life savings from uninsured ACC bonds sold through Lincoln's branches, often without customers understanding the distinction from insured deposits. Keating was convicted of fraud and racketeering but served only **4.5 years** after convictions were overturned on technicalities and he pleaded to lesser charges.

### Empire Savings and Loan (1984) — Pure fraud

Empire S&L in Mesquite, Texas holds the distinction of being the **first S&L closed specifically due to fraud**. Operating from a strip mall, it grew explosively to $309 million in assets through a criminal land-flip scheme: developer Danny Faulkner orchestrated chains of insider sales — sometimes **eight in a single day** — inflating land values by up to 800%, with Empire financing **110% loans at 18–19% interest**. Nearly 90% of deposits came from brokers attracted by above-market rates backed by FSLIC insurance. The seizure in March 1984 cost **$300 million** and left the government custodian of over **3,000 condominiums**, many eventually bulldozed. Approximately **120 participants were prosecuted and convicted**. Empire served as the canary in the coal mine for the broader S&L crisis.

The S&L crisis ultimately destroyed **1,043 thrift institutions** holding **$519 billion in assets** between 1986 and 1995. The Resolution Trust Corporation resolved 747 failed thrifts, and the total cost to taxpayers reached approximately **$124 billion**. The Department of Justice sent **1,706 bankers to prison** — a level of criminal accountability never replicated in subsequent crises.

## The 2000s: subprime lending destroys the system

The 2008 financial crisis produced over 480 bank failures between 2008 and 2013, but two institutions stand out for their scale and the clarity with which they illustrate operational failure in mortgage lending.

### Washington Mutual (2008) — Operational

Washington Mutual remains the **largest bank failure in U.S. history** — **$307 billion in assets**, 2,239 branches, 43,198 employees. The causal chain is a textbook case of deliberate risk escalation: CEO Kerry Killinger transformed WaMu from a conservative thrift into what he called "the Wal-Mart of Banking," shifting from 64% fixed-rate originations in 2003 to **55% high-risk products** (Option ARMs, subprime, home equity) by 2006. Between 2004 and 2007, WaMu underwrote **$184.8 billion in Option ARMs** — loans where borrowers could choose minimum payments that didn't even cover interest, causing balances to grow. By year-end 2007, **84% of Option ARMs were negatively amortizing**.

Internal fraud was rampant and deliberately ignored. A 2005 investigation of two California branches found fraud rates of **58% and 83%** — fabricated documents, phony identities, cut-and-pasted bank statements. Senior management was informed but took no meaningful action. Loan officers were compensated purely on volume, with bonuses for overcharging borrowers. The Chief Risk Officer had 35% of pay tied to growth and only 25% to risk management.

The housing collapse exposed these vulnerabilities. After Lehman Brothers' bankruptcy on September 15, 2008, S&P downgraded WaMu to junk, triggering a **$16.7 billion bank run over 9 days**. OTS seized the bank on September 25, 2008, and the FDIC sold it to JPMorgan Chase for **$1.9 billion** — resolving the largest bank failure in history at **zero cost to the FDIC insurance fund**. Killinger received over **$103 million in compensation** from 2003 to 2008; his replacement, Alan Fishman, kept an **$11.6 million severance** after serving less than three weeks.

### IndyMac (2008) — Operational/Liquidity

IndyMac Bancorp ($32 billion in assets) specialized in Alt-A "liar loans" — mortgages with little to no income documentation. Loan originations tripled from $22 billion in 2003 to **$90 billion in 2006**, making IndyMac the seventh-largest mortgage originator nationally. When the secondary market froze in late 2007, IndyMac was stuck with **$10.7 billion in unsaleable loans** and increasingly dependent on volatile brokered deposits (37% of total) and FHLB advances (**32–34% of total assets**).

OTS allowed IndyMac to retroactively revise Q1 2008 capital reports, counting an **$18 million note receivable that didn't exist** to keep the bank above the "well-capitalized" threshold. Senator Charles Schumer's June 26, 2008 public letter warning of "possible collapse" triggered a run: **$1.55 billion withdrawn in 11 business days**. Images of panicked depositors lining up outside branches — the first such scenes since the Great Depression — broadcast nationally and directly precipitated the subsequent run on Washington Mutual.

IndyMac's resolution cost the FDIC an estimated **$12.3 billion** — the most expensive in FDIC history. The bank was eventually sold to a consortium led by Steven Mnuchin (later Treasury Secretary) for $13.65 billion, rebranded as OneWest Bank. Approximately 10,000 uninsured depositors lost over **$270 million**.

## Fraud as a distinct failure vector

While most bank failures involve some element of mismanagement, several notable cases demonstrate how deliberate fraud can destroy institutions independent of macroeconomic conditions.

### Colonial Bank (2009) — Fraud

Colonial Bank ($25 billion in assets, Montgomery, Alabama) was brought down by a **$2.9 billion fraud scheme** orchestrated by Taylor, Bean & Whitaker Mortgage Corp. with the active complicity of Colonial insiders. Starting in 2002, TBW chronically overdrew its Colonial Bank accounts. When overdrafts exceeded $100 million by 2003, TBW Chairman Lee Farkas and Colonial SVP Catherine Kissick devised "Plan B": TBW sold Colonial fictitious mortgage loans — assets that either didn't exist or had already been sold to Freddie Mac. Over seven years, TBW fraudulently placed over **$1.5 billion in worthless assets** on Colonial's books. Neither PricewaterhouseCoopers (external auditor) nor Crowe Horwath (internal auditor) detected the fraud.

The scheme unraveled when FBI agents raided TBW in August 2009. Colonial was seized on August 14, 2009 and acquired by BB&T. The cost to the FDIC reached approximately **$3 billion**. Farkas was convicted of 14 counts and sentenced to **30 years in prison** — one of the few major criminal prosecutions from the financial crisis era. PwC was found liable for professional negligence and settled for **$335 million**.

### Superior Bank (2001) — Fraud/Accounting

Superior Bank ($2.3 billion, Hinsdale, Illinois), owned by the **Pritzker family** and investor Alvin Dworman, pioneered the subprime securitization strategies that would later devastate the financial system. From 1993 onward, Superior aggressively originated subprime mortgages and auto loans, securitizing them and retaining the riskiest residual tranches. Management used wildly optimistic valuation assumptions to inflate the book value of these residuals, which exceeded **300% of Tier 1 capital** — seven times more than any other savings institution.

Ernst & Young gave clean audit opinions annually from 1995 to 1999 while simultaneously earning twice as much from consulting to Superior as from its audit work. When regulators finally forced proper valuation in early 2001, a **$270 million write-down** (later growing to $420 million) rendered the bank insolvent. The Pritzkers refused to recapitalize, and OTS seized the bank in July 2001. The family settled with the FDIC for **$460 million** but admitted no liability and faced no criminal charges — despite having extracted over **$200 million in dividends** during the 1990s. E&Y settled for $125 million.

### First National Bank of Keystone (1999) — Fraud

Perhaps the most brazen fraud case, Keystone was a tiny West Virginia community bank (population 500) that ballooned from $100 million to **$1.1 billion** through a subprime securitization program in which every one of 19 deals was unprofitable. Management concealed losses by embezzling at least **$25 million** through bogus "due diligence fees" and falsifying regulatory reports. When OCC examiners discovered a **$515 million discrepancy**, management hired "security guards" to intimidate examiners and **literally buried three truckloads of bank records** using a backhoe. The FBI later excavated the evidence. The cost to the FDIC: **$750–$850 million** — from a bank in a town of 500 people.

## The 2023 crisis: social media accelerates bank runs

The March 2023 banking crisis demonstrated that digital banking and social media had compressed the timeline of bank runs from weeks to hours, creating a fundamentally new risk environment for institutions with concentrated, uninsured deposit bases.

### Silicon Valley Bank (2023) — Liquidity/Operational

SVB's collapse on March 10, 2023 was the **fastest major bank failure in history** — **$42 billion withdrawn in a single day**, with another $100 billion queued for the next morning. The 16th-largest U.S. bank ($209 billion in assets) served the tech and venture capital ecosystem, with **94% of deposits uninsured**. The root vulnerability was a massive duration mismatch: SVB invested deposit inflows in long-dated government bonds and MBS, accumulating **$17.7 billion in unrealized losses** as the Fed raised rates — nearly equal to the bank's entire equity capital. Management compounded the error by **unwinding all interest rate hedges** in 2022. When SVB announced a $1.8 billion loss on securities sales and a $2.25 billion capital raise on March 8, prominent VC investors advised portfolio companies to pull deposits, and social media amplified the panic overnight.

The FDIC, Treasury, and Federal Reserve invoked a **systemic risk exception** to protect all depositors. First-Citizens Bank acquired SVB's assets at a **$16.5 billion discount**. The estimated cost to the FDIC: **$20 billion**. The Federal Reserve created the Bank Term Funding Program, offering loans at par value of collateral — an implicit acknowledgment that unrealized losses threatened multiple institutions.

### Signature Bank (2023) — Contagion

Signature Bank ($110 billion, New York) was the purest contagion casualty. While it had genuine vulnerabilities — **90% uninsured deposits**, heavy crypto-industry exposure (27% of deposits at peak), and rapid 134% asset growth from 2019–2021 — the proximate cause was SVB's seizure. Customers withdrew **over $10 billion on March 10 alone**, depleting cash reserves and leaving a negative Federal Reserve balance. By the time NYSDFS closed it on March 12, available liquidity stood at just **$3 billion — 4% of deposits**. Flagstar Bank (NYCB subsidiary) acquired the deposits. Cost to FDIC: **$2.5 billion**.

### First Republic Bank (2023) — Contagion/Operational

First Republic ($229 billion) experienced a slower death — seven weeks of bleeding rather than SVB's 44 hours — that illustrates how contagion can destroy even well-regarded institutions. The San Francisco bank served wealthy clients with low-rate mortgages funded by their large (68% uninsured) deposits. This model created approximately **$27 billion in unrealized mortgage losses** when rates rose. SVB's failure triggered a **$25 billion single-day deposit outflow** on March 10. An unprecedented **$30 billion consortium deposit** from 11 major banks on March 16 bought time but couldn't reverse the damage. First Republic's Q1 earnings revealed **$100+ billion in total deposit losses**, triggering a second wave of flight. JPMorgan Chase acquired the bank on May 1 for **$10.6 billion**, making it the **second-largest bank failure in U.S. history**. Cost to FDIC: **$15.6 billion**.

## Patterns that persist across every era

A comparative view of all 12 failures reveals five structural patterns that recur regardless of decade or macroeconomic environment.

**First, concentrated and uninsured deposit bases are the single most reliable predictor of vulnerability.** Continental Illinois (85% wholesale), SVB (94% uninsured), Signature (90%), First Republic (68%), and IndyMac (heavy brokered deposits) all depended on funding sources that could vanish overnight. The 2023 crisis proved that digital banking has made this vulnerability existential: SVB's $42 billion single-day outflow dwarfed WaMu's $16.7 billion over nine days in 2008.

**Second, concentration risk in assets compounds concentration risk in funding.** Continental Illinois concentrated in energy loans from a single source. WaMu and IndyMac concentrated in mortgage products. SVB concentrated in long-duration bonds. Lincoln S&L concentrated in speculative real estate. Colonial concentrated its warehouse lending in a single counterparty. In every case, the institution bet its survival on a single sector or strategy.

**Third, regulators consistently identify problems years before failure but fail to force remediation.** The FDIC flagged Signature Bank's liquidity issues in 2019 — four years before it failed. The San Francisco FHLB recommended seizing Lincoln S&L in 1987 — two years before it was actually closed. SVB had **31 unaddressed supervisory warnings** at the time of failure. OTS allowed IndyMac to backdate capital reports. The pattern of regulatory identification followed by insufficient escalation has persisted across every era and every regulatory framework.

**Fourth, fraud-driven failures produce losses vastly disproportionate to institutional size.** Keystone — a $1.1 billion bank in a town of 500 — cost the FDIC up to $850 million. Empire S&L's $309 million in assets generated $300 million in losses. These cases demonstrate that fraud short-circuits normal risk-return relationships and that auditor failures (Ernst & Young at Superior, PwC at Colonial, KPMG at the 2023 trio) are often necessary enablers.

**Fifth, contagion chains follow predictable paths from the specific to the general.** Penn Square (1982) killed Continental Illinois (1984). IndyMac (July 2008) accelerated WaMu's run (September 2008). Silvergate's liquidation (March 8, 2023) primed panic at Signature; SVB's seizure (March 10) triggered Signature's collapse (March 12) and First Republic's fatal run. Each contagion chain moved from institutions with the most obvious vulnerabilities to those with superficially similar profiles.

## Conclusion: speed has changed, but the playbook hasn't

The most striking finding across 40 years of bank failures is the persistence of identical failure patterns despite successive waves of regulatory reform. Dodd-Frank, FIRREA, FDICIA, and the 2018 EGRRCPA rollback each responded to the most recent crisis while leaving the banking system vulnerable to the next variant. The 2023 failures demonstrated that **the velocity of bank runs has fundamentally changed** — social media and mobile banking compress what once took weeks into hours — but the underlying causes remain the same: asset-liability mismatches, concentrated funding, inadequate risk management, and regulatory lag.

The criminal accountability gap has widened dramatically. The S&L crisis produced **1,706 prison sentences**. The 2008 crisis produced essentially none for senior bank executives (the Colonial Bank/TBW prosecution being a notable exception). The 2023 failures have produced zero criminal cases. Meanwhile, the cost per failure has escalated: SVB alone cost the FDIC more than the entire Continental Illinois bailout, adjusted for inflation.

The central lesson is structural, not behavioral. Banks fail when they concentrate risk — in assets, in funding sources, in geographies, or in counterparties — and then encounter a shock that tests precisely that concentration. Every institution in this analysis believed its specific concentration was manageable. Every one was wrong.
