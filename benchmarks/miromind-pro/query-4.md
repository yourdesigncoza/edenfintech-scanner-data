Below is a focused set of regional/large‑regional U.S. bank failures over roughly the last 40 years, categorized by dominant failure mode, with timing pattern, warning signs, and outcomes.

---

## 1. Bank of New England (New England regional franchise)

- **Year of failure:** 1991  
- **Category:** **Operational** (credit‑risk and concentration management failure)  
- **Failure pattern:** **Gradual** – risk built over years; resolution in 1991  

**Warning signs**

- Extremely high concentration in risky commercial real estate: about **36.7% of total loans** were in this sector, far above regional peers, leaving the bank heavily exposed to a localized real‑estate downturn [1].  
- Aggressive growth via acquisitions across New England (Connecticut, Maine, Rhode Island) from mid‑1980s onward, without adequate diversification or integration of risk controls [1].  
- Weak oversight of subsidiaries and poor cost control; even as management cut payroll and sold subsidiaries, new bad loans continued to surface [1].  
- By 1990, Federal Reserve and other analyses described it as a “classic lesson in the failure to diversify risk,” highlighting structural vulnerability to regional shocks [1].

**Outcome**

- In January 1991, federal authorities seized three major banking subsidiaries of Bank of New England Corporation; the holding company became, in effect, a ward of the state pending sale [1].  
- The franchise was broken up and sold to other institutions under FDIC supervision.

**Key takeaway:**  
A prolonged, **credit‑driven, operational failure**: high concentration in a single risky asset class (commercial real estate) plus weak strategic and subsidiary oversight created a slow‑moving solvency problem that ended in regulatory seizure.

## 2. Washington Mutual (WaMu)

- **Year of failure:** 2008  
- **Category:** **Liquidity** (run on deposits triggered by deep credit losses)  
- **Failure pattern:** **Binary** – confidence collapse and run over ≈10 days  

**Warning signs**

- Strategic pivot since late 1990s to higher‑risk mortgages: subprime, option ARMs, and other non‑traditional products, often via acquisitions (e.g., Long Beach Mortgage), creating a large, low‑quality loan book [2].  
- Heavy geographic and product concentration in housing markets that later crashed; by mid‑2008, mortgage losses exceeded **\$6 billion**, sharply eroding capital [2].  
- Deteriorating performance and rising loss provisions through 2007–2008 signaled increasing stress [2].  
- In **September 2008**, a credit rating downgrade triggered depositor and market concern; over **\$16.7 billion** in deposits (about 9% of total) fled in nine days [2].

**Outcome**

- On **September 25, 2008**, the Office of Thrift Supervision placed WaMu into FDIC receivership; FDIC immediately sold the banking operations (about **\$307 billion in assets** and **\$188 billion in deposits**) to JPMorgan Chase for **\$1.9 billion** [2].  
- The holding company, Washington Mutual, Inc., filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy; residual assets and liabilities were worked out over several years, ultimately emerging as WMI Holdings in 2012 [2].

**Key takeaway:**  
A classically **binary liquidity failure on top of a long‑running credit problem**: once confidence broke after the downgrade, uninsured and nervous depositors ran rapidly, overwhelming a bank already weakened by years of subprime‑driven capital erosion.

## 3. IndyMac Bank

- **Year of failure:** 2008  
- **Category:** **Operational** (underwriting/funding model failure, with a final liquidity run)  
- **Failure pattern:** **Binary end‑stage**, preceded by a **gradual deterioration** in loan quality and capital  

**Warning signs**

- Business model centered on aggressive origination and securitization of **Alt‑A and nontraditional mortgages** (option ARMs, low‑doc/no‑doc loans) with weak income/asset verification and questionable appraisals [3].  
- Very high concentration in residential real estate in **California and Florida**, amplifying regional housing risk [3].  
- Funding heavily reliant on **brokered deposits** and Federal Home Loan Bank advances – inherently more flighty and cost‑sensitive [3].  
- By **March 2008**, non‑performing loans and MBS downgrades were materially undermining capital; the May 2008 10‑Q warned of capital inadequacy and dependence on brokered deposits [3].  
- Public letters from Senator Charles Schumer to regulators in June 2008 highlighting IndyMac’s risks sparked depositor concern; a **\$1.3 billion** deposit run followed [3].

**Outcome**

- On **July 11, 2008**, the OTS closed IndyMac and the FDIC created **IndyMac Federal Bank, FSB** as a bridge bank to assume insured deposits and certain assets; about \$32 billion in assets were involved [3].  
- Roughly 10,000 depositors had balances above the then‑\$100,000 insurance cap; FDIC advanced 50% of uninsured balances as a “special advance dividend” and ultimately raised the general cap to **\$250,000** retroactively via Dodd‑Frank [3].  
- Most business assets were sold to IMB HoldCo, which became **OneWest Bank** [3].

**Key takeaway:**  
A textbook **operational risk failure** (lax underwriting, product and geographic concentration, unstable funding) that **ended in a swift run** once the market became aware of the embedded losses and fragile capital.

## 4. First NBC Bank (New Orleans)

- **Year of failure:** 2017  
- **Category:** **Operational** (governance, concentration risk, and complexity)  
- **Failure pattern:** **Gradual** – repeated supervisory warnings over years  

**Warning signs**

- A **dominant CEO with broad lending authority** and limited effective board oversight, leading to outsized, complex lending relationships and concentrations [4].  
- Rapid asset growth funded by **high‑cost deposits**, compressing net interest margin and leaving little buffer for losses [4].  
- Large and complex exposures in **trade receivables and tax‑credit investments**, for which the bank lacked strong risk‑management controls [4].  
- Multiple full‑scope examinations (2006–2017) by FDIC and the Louisiana Office of Financial Institutions identified **asset‑quality, concentration, and governance problems**; four separate Board Resolutions from 2009–2015 attempted remediation but proved ineffective [4].  
- Supervisors concluded that a stronger enforcement action was warranted as early as 2010 but was not implemented; capital injections temporarily masked underlying problems [4].  

**Outcome**

- On **April 28, 2017**, the Louisiana OFI closed First NBC and appointed the FDIC as receiver; closing assets were about **\$4 billion** [4].  
- The FDIC arranged a purchase‑and‑assumption transaction, with Whitney Bank acquiring most deposits and assets. Estimated loss to the Deposit Insurance Fund (DIF) was around **\$997 million** [4].

**Key takeaway:**  
A **slow‑burn operational and governance failure**—concentrated, complex positions plus ineffective board oversight and delayed regulatory escalation—until losses and capital erosion forced closure.

## 5. Washington Federal Bank for Savings (Chicago)

- **Year of failure:** 2017  
- **Category:** **Fraud** (loan‑book manipulation and regulatory deception)  
- **Failure pattern:** **Binary** – failure quickly after fraud uncovered  

**Warning signs**

- By the time of failure the bank had at least **\$66 million in non‑performing loans**, disproportionate to its modest balance sheet (≈\$166 million in assets) [5].  
- OCC examiners found substantial dissipation of assets and “unsafe or unsound” practices.  
- Two board members (and another previously) later pleaded guilty to **conspiracy to make false entries in bank records** and to **deceive the OCC** during its examination: they falsified data so the bank would appear compliant with rules and internal policies [5].  
- Internal whistleblower complaints and, soon after, the CEO’s death intensified scrutiny, revealing the underlying asset problems [6].

**Outcome**

- Less than two weeks after the CEO’s death, regulators closed the bank in December 2017 and the FDIC sold insured deposits and **\$23.7 million** of assets to Royal Savings Bank [6].  
- About 8% of deposits were uninsured; those customers’ recovery depended on the receivership’s asset disposition. The estimated DIF loss was **\$60.5 million** [6].  
- Multiple former board members received federal prison sentences and fines for their role in falsifying records and obstructing supervision [5].

**Key takeaway:**  
This is a clear **fraud‑driven regional thrift failure**: supervisory “red flags” (non‑performing loans, suspicious recordkeeping) escalated rapidly once deception was uncovered, leading to a swift closure.

## 6. Silicon Valley Bank (SVB)

- **Year of failure:** 2023  
- **Category:** **Liquidity** (interest‑rate risk crystallizing as a deposit run)  
- **Failure pattern:** **Binary** – essentially a 48‑hour collapse  

**Warning signs**

- Exceptionally high reliance on **uninsured deposits**—over **90%** of balances—largely from tech and venture‑capital firms, creating classic run risk [7].  
- Aggressive investment of deposits in longer‑duration fixed‑rate securities; as rates rose in 2022, SVB accrued large **unrealized losses** on available‑for‑sale securities that were visible to markets and regulators [7].  
- Supervisors had previously flagged **interest‑rate risk, liquidity‑risk management, and concentration** of funding in a small group of large, uninsured depositors [7].  
- On **March 8, 2023**, SVB announced a securities sale and capital raise to cover losses, crystallizing market fears. Within **March 9–10**, depositors attempted to withdraw **\$142 billion** (actual **\$42 billion** outflows and another \$100 billion queued), nearly half the deposit base in 48 hours [7].

**Outcome**

- California regulators closed SVB on **March 10, 2023**; the FDIC created a **Deposit Insurance National Bank** and then a bridge bank structure.  
- Because of contagion risk, U.S. authorities used a **“systemic risk” exception** to guarantee **all** deposits (insured and uninsured) at SVB (and later Signature) to stem broader runs [7].

**Key takeaway:**  
SVB is a prototypical **binary liquidity failure in a digital‑run era**: idiosyncratic interest‑rate and concentration risks were visible, but once confidence snapped, tech‑savvy clients withdrew at unprecedented speed.

## 7. Signature Bank (New York)

- **Year of failure:** 2023  
- **Category:** **Liquidity** (run, heavily influenced by **contagion** from SVB)  
- **Failure pattern:** **Binary** – collapsed within days of SVB  

**Warning signs**

- Heavy reliance on **uninsured, business, and crypto‑related deposits**; over **90%** of deposits were uninsured, similar to SVB [7].  
- Significant exposure to **cryptocurrency clients** and digital‑asset platforms, which were themselves under stress, heightening perceived risk.  
- Supervisors had noted **liquidity and management weaknesses** even before 2023 (e.g., funding‑concentration and governance issues) [7].  
- After SVB’s collapse, depositors rapidly pulled funds from Signature; withdrawals surged in the intervening days, leaving the bank illiquid [7].

**Outcome**

- New York’s Department of Financial Services closed Signature on **March 12, 2023**; the FDIC took over and created a bridge‑bank.  
- As with SVB, authorities invoked a **systemic risk exception** to guarantee all deposits. Most of the business was then acquired by New York Community Bancorp (via Signature Bridge Bank) [7].

**Key takeaway:**  
Signature’s failure shows how **liquidity risk at regional banks can be amplified by sectoral exposure (crypto) and contagion**—even without obvious capital insolvency at the time of the run.

## 8. First Republic Bank (California)

- **Year of failure:** 2023  
- **Category:** **Contagion** (confidence shock from prior regional failures, plus liquidity stress)  
- **Failure pattern:** **Binary** – swift outflows following SVB and Signature  

**Warning signs**

- High share (≈**70%**) of **uninsured deposits**, especially from high‑net‑worth clients and businesses, mirroring the risk profile of SVB and Signature [7].  
- Concentration in affluent coastal markets with large, sophisticated depositor base; once peers failed, these clients reacted quickly.  
- Significant interest‑rate and market‑value risk on longer‑dated assets; rising rates squeezed margins and increased unrealized losses, undermining market confidence.  
- After SVB and Signature failed, First Republic experienced massive outflows (tens of billions of dollars) despite a \$30 billion deposit infusion from large U.S. banks, indicating a loss of depositor trust [7].

**Outcome**

- California regulators closed First Republic in early May 2023; the FDIC conducted a competitive bidding process.  
- **JPMorgan Chase** acquired substantially all deposits and most assets under the **least‑cost test**, without invoking a systemic‑risk exception, and all depositors were protected [7].

**Key takeaway:**  
First Republic illustrates **contagion as a primary failure driver**: a bank can be solvent on paper but still fail if peer failures rapidly shift depositor and market perceptions.

## 9. Pulaski Savings Bank (Chicago)

- **Year of failure:** 2025  
- **Category:** **Fraud** (suspected internal fraud leading to capital impairment)  
- **Failure pattern:** **Binary** – closure immediately after serious irregularities surfaced  

**Warning signs**

- Illinois regulators (IDFPR) determined Pulaski was in an **“unsafe and unsound condition”** with an **impaired capital position** [8].  
- FDIC explicitly cited **suspected fraud** as the reason the estimated loss to the Deposit Insurance Fund (DIF) was unusually high relative to the bank’s size [8].  
- As of September 30 prior to failure, the bank had a small footprint—about **\$49.5 million in assets** and **\$42.7 million in deposits**—suggesting a localized, internal‑control breakdown rather than macroeconomic stress [8].

**Outcome**

- On **January 17, 2025**, IDFPR closed Pulaski and appointed the FDIC as receiver.  
- **Millennium Bank** assumed all deposits (for a 4.61% premium) and acquired roughly **\$45 million** of assets; FDIC retained about **\$4.5 million** of assets for later sale [8].  
- The estimated DIF loss was about **\$28.5 million**, attributed in part to suspected fraud [8].

**Key takeaway:**  
Pulaski is a small but recent example of a **fraud‑driven, idiosyncratic failure**, with little broader contagion because the cause was clearly internal and localized.

## Cross‑Case Patterns in Regional Bank Failures

Synthesizing across these cases:

### By category

- **Operational (credit / governance / risk‑management)**  
  - Bank of New England, IndyMac, First NBC  
  - Patterns: multi‑year build‑up of risky concentration (real estate, tax credits, complex corporate credits); dominant executives; weak boards; repeated but under‑enforced supervisory findings.  
  - Typical trajectory: **gradual** deterioration in asset quality and earnings → capital erosion → eventual liquidity stress.

- **Liquidity (pure or dominant driver)**  
  - Washington Mutual, SVB, Signature  
  - Patterns: heavy reliance on **uninsured** or **volatile** funding (wholesale, brokered, sector‑concentrated), large unrealized losses or negative news shocks.  
  - Trajectory: often **binary** in the final phase – a trigger event (rating downgrade, capital raise announcement, peer failure) sparks a rapid run.

- **Contagion**  
  - First Republic  
  - Pattern: banks with business models similar to a failed peer (uninsured, concentrated deposit base; visible interest‑rate risk) are highly vulnerable once confidence breaks industry‑wide.

- **Fraud**  
  - Washington Federal Bank for Savings, Pulaski Savings  
  - Pattern: localized failures, often of smaller institutions, where **internal controls and external auditing fail** and management actively falsifies records. Once detected, closure is swift and binary.

- **Regulatory**  
  - In these examples, regulation rarely appears as the *primary* cause. Instead, **regulatory lapses exacerbate operational problems** (e.g., delayed strong actions at First NBC) or struggle to keep pace with rapidly evolving liquidity risk (e.g., SVB).

### Gradual vs. binary patterns

- **Gradual failures:**  
  - Bank of New England, IndyMac, First NBC  
  - Common features: risk concentrations, weak governance, complex investments. Early but sometimes muted warning signs in exam reports and financial ratios.

- **Binary failures:**  
  - Washington Mutual, Washington Federal, SVB, Signature, First Republic, Pulaski  
  - Common features: sudden **information or confidence shock** (downgrades, fraud revelations, peer failures), followed by **fast deposit‑flight** or immediate regulatory closure.

### Recurrent warning signs to monitor in regional banks

From these cases, the highest‑value early‑warning indicators are:

1. **Funding structure**
   - Share of **uninsured deposits** and concentration in specific depositor segments (VC, crypto, high‑net‑worth, municipal).  
   - Heavy reliance on **brokered deposits** or short‑term wholesale funding.

2. **Asset concentration and complexity**
   - Outsized exposures to a single sector (commercial real estate, a niche mortgage product, tax‑credit investments).  
   - Rapid asset growth in complex, hard‑to‑value areas (Alt‑A/option ARMs, tax credits, trade receivables).

3. **Interest‑rate and market‑value risk**
   - Large, unhedged, fixed‑rate securities or loan portfolios with material **unrealized losses** relative to capital.  
   - Poor or opaque asset‑liability management disclosures.

4. **Governance and culture**
   - **Dominant CEO** with little effective board challenge.  
   - Recurrent “matters requiring board attention” in exam reports without credible remediation.  
   - High turnover in risk and finance functions.

5. **Transparency and regulatory interaction**
   - Late, incomplete, or inconsistent regulatory filings.  
   - Evidence of **backdating**, misclassification of assets, or disputes with examiners.  
   - Any sign of falsified records or obstruction (as in Washington Federal and Pulaski) is an immediate red flag.

6. **External signals**
   - Rating downgrades, sharp widening of funding spreads, or difficulty rolling over wholesale funding.  
   - Abrupt capital‑raising attempts or asset sales at large discounts.  
   - Sector‑specific shocks affecting peer institutions (e.g., crypto, VC, regional real‑estate downturns).

### How to use these patterns

For risk analysis or early‑warning monitoring of today’s regional banks:

- **Map each bank’s profile** to these failure archetypes:  
  - High uninsured deposits + long‑duration securities → SVB/Signature/WaMu‑type liquidity risk.  
  - Concentrated CRE or esoteric credit exposures + weak governance → Bank of New England/First NBC archetype.  
  - Smaller, fast‑growing institutions with opaque loan books → Washington Federal/Pulaski fraud‑risk archetype.

- **Track both slow and fast variables:**  
  - Slow: concentration ratios, non‑performing loan trends, capital ratios, supervisory findings.  
  - Fast: deposit flight data (where available), social‑media narratives, rating changes, and capital‑market pricing.

- **Assess contagion channels:**  
  - Banks that share depositor segments, business models, or geographic footprints with a stressed peer are more likely to experience **binary‑style** failures if confidence is lost.

These historic cases demonstrate that while credit and governance failures typically brew over years, **regional banks actually die from runs and loss of confidence**—often within days—once markets and depositors fully perceive those embedded weaknesses.
