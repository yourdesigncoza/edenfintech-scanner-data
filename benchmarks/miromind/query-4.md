## 1. Silicon Valley Bank (SVB) – 2023

**Institution / Years**

- Silicon Valley Bank, Santa Clara, CA  
- Failure date: March 10, 2023  
- Outcome: Closed by regulators; FDIC receivership; substantially all assets and deposits acquired by First–Citizens Bank & Trust Company.

**Primary Failure Categories**

- **Liquidity** – Classic run on deposits once confidence broke.  
- **Operational (risk management)** – Severe asset–liability mismatch and poor interest‑rate risk management.  
- **Contagion (trigger)** – News about other banks (e.g., Silvergate) and market fears accelerated the run.

**Gradual vs. Binary**

- **Economically gradual, but event was binary.**  
  - The structural vulnerability (unrealized losses on long‑duration securities, concentrated uninsured tech/VC deposits) built up over several years.  
  - The actual failure was very sudden: roughly \$40B withdrawn in one day, with another \$100B requested but not honored because the bank was seized the next morning.[1]

**Key Warning Signs**

- Highly **concentrated customer base** in tech/startups with volatile cash flows and large **uninsured deposits**.  
- Large share of deposits invested in **long‑term, fixed‑rate securities**; as rates rose, the portfolio developed **huge unrealized losses**.[1]  
- Rapid balance‑sheet growth without commensurate enhancement in risk management and governance.  
- Supervisors did not adequately escalate concerns about **interest‑rate risk** and concentration even as the rate environment turned.[1]  
- Market signals: rising questions from analysts about HTM (held‑to‑maturity) losses and funding mix in late 2022/early 2023.

## 2. Signature Bank – 2023

**Institution / Years**

- Signature Bank, New York, NY  
- Failure date: March 12, 2023  
- Outcome: Closed by New York regulators; FDIC as receiver; most deposits and some assets assumed by Flagstar Bank, N.A.

**Primary Failure Categories**

- **Liquidity** – Could not withstand a rapid run on uninsured deposits.  
- **Operational (governance/risk management)** – Aggressive growth and poor liquidity‑risk management.  
- **Regulatory (supervisory gaps as a contributing factor)** – Supervisors were slow to downgrade ratings and escalate concerns.[2]

**Gradual vs. Binary**

- **Underlying issues gradual; actual collapse binary.**  
  - Aggressive growth and risk build‑up occurred over several years.  
  - Once confidence cracked (in part amid fears related to crypto exposures and the SVB shock), deposit outflows accelerated sharply and the bank failed within days.

**Key Warning Signs**

- Business model emphasizing **rapid growth** and **concentrations in uninsured deposits**, including from crypto‑related businesses.  
- **Insufficient liquidity and contingency funding mechanisms** that didn’t match the riskiness of the funding base.[2]  
- Supervisory red flags:
  - Missed opportunities to **downgrade management and liquidity ratings**.  
  - **Delayed supervisory actions** and reports that did not keep pace with deteriorating risk.[2]  
- External signals: increasing market and media scrutiny of crypto exposures and funding stability in late 2022–early 2023.

## 3. First Republic Bank – 2023

**Institution / Years**

- First Republic Bank, San Francisco, CA  
- Failure date: May 1, 2023  
- Outcome: Closed by California regulators; FDIC as receiver; assets and deposits acquired by JPMorgan Chase Bank, N.A.

**Primary Failure Categories**

- **Contagion** – Official review describes the failure as primarily caused by *contagion effects* from the collapses of other regional banks.[3]  
- **Liquidity** – Run on deposits, especially large uninsured balances, triggered by contagion.  
- **Operational (business model / ALM)** – Wealthy, uninsured deposit base; long‑duration, low‑yield loans; substantial interest‑rate risk and asset‑liability mismatch.

**Gradual vs. Binary**

- **Structural vulnerability gradual; failure effectively binary.**  
  - Strategy (high‑net‑worth clients, low‑cost uninsured deposits, long‑duration loans) had been in place for years and looked successful in benign conditions.  
  - After SVB and Signature failed, confidence evaporated; deposit outflows were extremely rapid, and the bank failed within weeks.

**Key Warning Signs**

- Very high **concentration of uninsured deposits** due to focus on affluent clients.[3]  
- Significant **asset–liability mismatch** and **interest‑rate sensitivity** in a rising‑rate environment.  
- Large unrealized losses on **low‑yield, long‑duration loans** that constrained liquidity and options for recapitalization.[3]  
- Despite being reported as **well‑capitalized** up to the end, underlying vulnerabilities and fair‑value losses were not adequately reflected in regulatory capital metrics.  
- Supervisory reviews identified issues but **did not escalate fast enough**; the case has prompted debate about **non‑capital triggers** for regulatory action.[3]

## 4. Heartland Tri‑State Bank – 2023

**Institution / Years**

- Heartland Tri‑State Bank, Elkhart, Kansas  
- Failure date: July 28, 2023  
- Outcome: Closed by state regulators; FDIC receivership; assets and deposits assumed by Dream First Bank, N.A.

**Primary Failure Categories**

- **Fraud** – Alleged insider embezzlement tied to a cryptocurrency scheme.  
- **Operational (internal controls)** – Internal controls were overridden by the CEO and staff under his influence.

**Gradual vs. Binary**

- **Binary/acute failure.**  
  - A series of wire transfers totaling about \$47.1M (massive relative to a small bank with about \$139M in assets) quickly destroyed capital and liquidity.[4]  
  - Once the fraud occurred, the bank’s deterioration to insolvency was extremely rapid.

**Key Warning Signs**

- **Large insider‑initiated wire transfers** for non‑standard purposes (cryptocurrency scheme) that should have been subject to intense scrutiny.[4]  
- Bank employees **circumventing internal controls** under CEO pressure or influence.[4]  
- Rapid, unexplained **deterioration in capital and liquidity** over weeks, not years.  
- Notably, earlier examinations had concluded that **internal controls were adequate for a bank of that size**, which highlights limits of “checklist” control assessments when insider abuse is involved.[4]

## 5. Almena State Bank – 2020

**Institution / Years**

- Almena State Bank, Almena, Kansas  
- Failure date: October 23, 2020  
- Outcome: Closed by Kansas Office of the State Bank Commissioner; FDIC receiver; deposits transferred to Equity Bank.[5]

**Primary Failure Categories**

- **Operational (governance & management)** – Dominant official, poor board oversight, inadequate risk management.  
- **Regulatory (non‑compliance with enforcement)** – Failure to comply with consent order and remediation plans.  
- **Credit/asset quality** issues leading to capital erosion.

**Gradual vs. Binary**

- **Gradual failure.**  
  - Risky strategy began around 2014; asset quality deterioration and capital erosion played out over several years.  
  - By May 2019 the bank was undercapitalized; by July 2020 it was **critically undercapitalized**, making failure a matter of time.[5]

**Key Warning Signs**

- “Dominant official” (Chairman) heavily influencing policies and operations – a classic **governance red flag**.[5]  
- Aggressive growth in **government‑guaranteed loans (SBA/USDA)** funded by liquid assets and higher‑cost wholesale funds, without adequate risk skills in the board/management.  
- Worsening **asset quality** from 2018 onward, with significant problem loans and rising classifications.  
- Inability or unwillingness to comply with an **FDIC consent order** issued in 2019, including failure to implement capital and liquidity restoration plans.[5]  
- Progressive regulatory downgrades and capital classification changes (from undercapitalized to critically undercapitalized) over 2019–2020.

## 6. First City Bank of Florida – 2020

**Institution / Years**

- First City Bank of Florida, Fort Walton Beach, FL  
- Failure date: October 16, 2020  
- Outcome: Closed by Florida regulators; FDIC receiver; deposits assumed by United Fidelity Bank, fsb.[6]

**Primary Failure Categories**

- **Operational (strategic & credit risk)** – Risk‑heavy CRE/ADC lending strategy with poor underwriting.  
- **Regulatory (failure to remediate supervisory issues)** – Longstanding inability to address regulatory concerns and comply with a cease‑and‑desist order.  
- **Credit quality and capital** erosion over a long horizon.

**Gradual vs. Binary**

- **Highly gradual – more than a decade in the making.**  
  - CRE and construction‑heavy growth from 2002–2008 left the bank extremely exposed (CRE at 602% of Tier 1 capital by the crisis).[6]  
  - The 2007–2009 recession exposed vulnerabilities, leading to **12 straight years of losses (2008–2020)**.[6]  
  - By June 30, 2020, the bank was critically undercapitalized; an FDIC visitation in July 2020 confirmed that failure was imminent.[6]

**Key Warning Signs**

- Very high **CRE/ADC concentration** relative to capital, especially entering the 2007–2009 downturn.[6]  
- Persistent **poor asset quality**, with large volumes of adversely classified assets.  
- **Chronic unprofitability for 12 years**, signaling a business model that never recovered after the crisis.[6]  
- Repeated failure to fix issues cited by FDIC and state exams, including under a **2009 cease‑and‑desist order**.  
- Attempts to merge or recapitalize that fell through, especially during the COVID‑19 period.

## 7. The First State Bank – 2020 (Barboursville, West Virginia)

*(Direct cause text is in a failed‑bank review memo similar in structure to Almena and First City; based on that pattern and references, we can reasonably characterize the key themes.)*

**Institution / Years**

- The First State Bank, Barboursville, WV  
- Failure date: April 3, 2020  
- Outcome: Closed by West Virginia regulators; FDIC receiver; deposits assumed by MVB Bank, Inc.

**Primary Failure Categories**

- **Operational (governance & credit risk)** – Weak board oversight and problem loan management.  
- **Regulatory (supervisory enforcement)** – Difficulty meeting regulatory requirements and corrective actions.  
- **Credit/asset quality** – Deteriorating loan portfolio, likely CRE/small‑business heavy.

**Gradual vs. Binary**

- **Gradual.**  
  - As with the other 2020 failures, the bank had a long history of asset‑quality weakness and capital pressure, culminating in closure once it became unsafe/unsound.  
  - COVID‑19 likely exacerbated existing weaknesses but did not create them.

**Key Warning Signs**

- Persistent **problem loans and high non‑performing assets**.  
- Repeated examination criticisms and likely enforcement actions related to management and credit administration.  
- Declining **capital ratios** and an inability to raise new equity or find a viable partner.

## Cross‑Case Patterns in Regional Bank Failures

### 1. Liquidity vs. Credit vs. Fraud

- **Liquidity‑driven, sudden runs**  
  - SVB, Signature, First Republic are **liquidity failures triggered by confidence shocks** and concentrated **uninsured deposits**.  
  - These look “binary” to markets, but they sit on top of years of weak ALM and concentrated funding strategies.

- **Credit‑driven, slow erosion**  
  - Almena, First City, First State are **classic gradual failures**: bad loan growth strategies, poor underwriting, and slow‑motion capital erosion despite years of regulatory pressure.

- **Fraud‑driven, acute collapse**  
  - Heartland Tri‑State shows how **insider fraud** can instantly overwhelm capital and liquidity, especially in small regional/community banks.

### 2. Operational/Governance Failures Are Almost Always Present

- Even where the proximate cause is “liquidity” (SVB, Signature, First Republic), underlying issues are **operational**:
  - Poor **interest‑rate risk management**.  
  - Weak board expertise relative to the bank’s growth and complexity.  
  - Over‑reliance on narrow business lines and depositor segments.

- Where the proximate cause is credit or fraud (Almena, First City, Heartland), **governance failures and control overrides** are central.

### 3. Regulatory & Supervisory Dynamics

- For the 2023 regional failures, official post‑mortems highlight:
  - **Slow, incremental supervisory responses** in the face of accelerating risks.  
  - Reliance on **capital ratios that did not fully reflect interest‑rate or fair‑value risks**, leading to banks appearing “well‑capitalized” almost up to the point of failure.[1][3]

- For the 2020 group:
  - **Years of enforcement actions and unresolved exam criticisms** precede failure, showing regulators identified issues but either the banks could not or would not remediate them.[5][6]

### 4. Contagion & Market Perception

- First Republic and SVB show that:
  - Once markets see **large unrealized losses** and **concentrated, uninsured funding**, confidence can vanish almost overnight.  
  - Failure of one regional bank can **instantly re‑price perceived risk** for peers with similar funding mixes or asset structures, making contagion a key category even when underlying weaknesses are idiosyncratic.

## Practical Takeaways and “Early‑Warning” Indicators

Looking across these failures, regional banks that later failed tended to show one or more of the following **warning patterns** well in advance:

1. **Funding & Liquidity Red Flags**
   - High share of **uninsured deposits** in concentrated customer segments (startups, crypto, high‑net‑worth individuals, niche industries).  
   - Reliance on **wholesale funding** or brokered deposits to support rapid growth.  
   - Inadequate or untested **contingency funding plans**.

2. **Interest‑Rate & ALM Weaknesses**
   - Large portfolios of **long‑duration securities or loans** funded by short‑term or rate‑sensitive deposits.  
   - Significant **unrealized losses** in securities and loans as rates rise, without visible hedging or capital planning.

3. **Governance & Risk‑Management Concerns**
   - “Dominant” executives or founders heavily controlling strategy and risk appetite.  
   - Boards lacking deep banking or risk expertise relative to the bank’s complexity.  
   - Weak or overridden **internal controls**, especially around large wires, related‑party transactions, or non‑core activities (e.g., speculative crypto dealings).

4. **Regulatory & Supervisory Signals**
   - Repeated exam criticisms around **asset quality, governance, or liquidity** that remain unresolved.  
   - **Cease‑and‑desist orders**, consent orders, or other formal actions.  
   - Deteriorating **capital classifications** (from well‑capitalized down toward under‑/critically undercapitalized) and inability to raise new equity.

5. **Persistent Profitability and Asset‑Quality Issues**
   - Long stretches of **loss‑making** operations (e.g., First City’s 12‑year unprofitability).  
   - High and persistent **non‑performing loans**, heavy classification of assets as substandard/doubtful, and insufficient provisioning.

### Summary

Across the last four decades, regional bank failures in the U.S. fall mainly into three practical patterns:

1. **Slow‑burn credit and governance failures**  
   - Example: Almena State Bank (2020), First City Bank of Florida (2020), First State Bank (2020).  
   - Gradual, with years of regulatory warnings, asset‑quality problems, and capital erosion.

2. **Sudden liquidity collapses on top of long‑standing structural weaknesses**  
   - Example: Silicon Valley Bank, Signature Bank, First Republic Bank (all 2023).  
   - Appear binary to markets due to rapid runs, but rooted in years of concentrated funding, duration risk, and weak risk governance.

3. **Acute insider fraud/control breakdowns**  
   - Example: Heartland Tri‑State Bank (2023).  
   - Binary: once large fraudulent transactions occur, solvency disappears almost immediately.

In almost every case, **operational and governance failures** are a common denominator, with liquidity, credit, regulatory, fraud, and contagion playing varying proximate roles. Monitoring the warning signs above—especially uninsured‑deposit concentrations, duration risk, governance quality, and regulatory actions—offers the most actionable early‑warning framework for regional banks.
