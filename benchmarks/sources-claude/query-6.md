## 1. Cyclical vs. Structurally Impairing?

**Primarily cyclical for survivors, structurally impairing for a predictable subset.**

The Sixth District Fed analysis (2014) found that of 480+ national failures in 2008–2013, **not all banks with high-risk characteristics failed**. The differentiator was risk management discipline and capital-raising speed, not just the risk profile itself. Banks that raised capital early survived at markedly higher rates than those delaying action — even with comparable CRE concentrations.

The MIT Sloan "Failing Banks" (2024) paper adds the academic framing: bank failures are "almost always and everywhere a deterioration of bank solvency" — predominantly fundamental, not panic-driven. Recovery rates in receivership above 75% correlate with run-driven failures (fundamentally solvent banks); below 50% correlate with structural insolvency. The implication: a bank with good fundamentals temporarily stressed by a cycle shock is recoverable. One with eroded asset quality is not.

**Structural impairment threshold:** CRE concentration >300% Tier 1 + loan growth 2x+ local GDP growth + noncore funding >30% = structural impairment, not cyclical stress.

## 2. Recovery Timeline

| Evidence | Finding |
|----------|---------|
| Sixth District GFC data (Fed Atlanta) | Survivors implemented risk management changes within 12–18 months of distress onset; capital raises peaked 2009–2011 |
| Western US community bank case (InterimExecs) | 12.5 months for operational turnaround; 2–3 years to full profitability ($16M → $74M net income) |
| TARP repayment data (Investigative Economics) | Most GFC-era TARP recipients repaid within 3–5 years (2009 → 2012–2014) |
| MIT Sloan / NY Fed research | Asset recovery rates in receivership average 52% of book value (pre-FDIC era); modern era recovery rates higher (80%+) for run-driven failures |

**Average for surviving institutions:** 12–18 months to operational stabilization; 2–4 years to full profitability. Equity price recovery longer (3–5 years).

## 3. Survivorship Data

**From Sixth District Fed analysis:**
- De novo bank failure rate (2000–2007 cohort): 14% nationally, **42% in Georgia**, 19% in Florida
- Banks with CRE concentration + noncore funding + rapid loan growth: 15.1% failure rate in Sixth District vs. national average

**TARP as survivorship proxy (GFC):** TARP access was effectively a binary survival signal — recipients survived or consolidated on favorable terms; denied applicants were forced into distressed sales (National City → PNC; LaSalle → BofA).

**Approximate 20-year survivorship:** No single published study gives a clean rate for distressed-but-surviving institutions. Triangulating: ~65–75% of banks that entered problem-bank status (CAMELS 4-5) ultimately survived through recovery or merger — not failure. The remainder failed or were acquired under duress. Survival rates are highly size- and capital-dependent.

### Case 1: Anonymous Western U.S. Community Bank — Goldman Sachs Recapitalization ✓

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Period** | 2009–2012 |
| **Trigger** | $100M+ troubled CRE/construction loans; $82M operating loss 2009; broad FDIC enforcement action |

## 5. Synthesis: What Separates Recoveries from Failures

From primary sources, three factors predict survivorship with the highest reliability:

**1. Capital access speed** — The GFC data shows capital-raisers in 2009 had markedly better outcomes than those who delayed to 2010–2011. Every quarter of delay reduces recovery probability. Modern screen: Is management actively pursuing capital? Are there institutional investors engaged?

**2. Operational triage (workout separation)** — The InterimExecs case study documents what most turnaround practitioners know: putting production lenders on workout duty kills both recovery and new business simultaneously. Banks that separated functions recovered faster. Screen: Does the bank have a dedicated workout/special assets team?

**3. Policy access** — TARP in GFC, FHLB/BTFP in 2023. Banks that could access emergency liquidity facilities survived. Those denied or ineligible didn't. Modern screen: FHLB membership + pre-pledged collateral capacity. If a bank is shrinking its FHLB borrowing capacity under stress, that is a kill signal.

**Kill Factor:** CRE concentration >300% Tier 1 + loan growth 2x GDP + brokered deposits >20% = Georgia/Florida de novo profile → structural impairment

**Friction Factor:** CRE 200–300% Tier 1 + sound capital ratios + active workout team → cyclical distress, PCS friction -1
