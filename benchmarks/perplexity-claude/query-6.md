## 1. Cyclical vs. Structurally Impairing?

**Bottom line: Primarily cyclical, but structural fragility determines survivorship.**

Regional bank downturns are driven by macro-financial cycles (credit booms → CRE crashes, rate shocks, deposit runs). The sector as a whole recovers after each wave:
- Post-S&L: 1,043 failures 1986–1995, yet surviving banks rebuilt capital ratios within a decade
- Post-GFC: 571 failures 2008–2011 peak; survivors' ROE returned to 9–11% by 2014
- Post-2023: 4 failures March–May 2023; systemic contagion contained; sector CRE charge-offs stayed at 0.25% by Q4 2024 [4]

**The structural impairment threshold:** Banks with two or more of these features convert cyclical stress into terminal impairment:
- Uninsured deposits >70% + peer failure triggering depositor coordination
- Unrealized securities losses >75% of CET1 (equity capital wiped out under marking-to-market)
- CRE concentration >400% of Tier 1 + severe geographic concentration
- Active fraud — by definition non-cyclical; recovery probability approaches zero

## 2. Average Recovery Timeline

| Evidence source | Finding |
|----------------|---------|
| GFC-era survivors (2008–11 distress) | Balance-sheet NPL normalization: 24–36 months from peak stress [4] |
| TARP repayment data | Most TARP-recipient regional banks repaid within 3–5 years (2009 recipients → 2012–2014) |
| 2023 wave survivors (NYCB, MTB, RF) | Operational stabilization: 12–18 months; CRE workout completion: 3–5 years [5] |
| Deposit run recovery (NY Fed research) | Deposit levels restored within one fiscal quarter (~3–4 months) after run subsides — IF bank survives [Phase A Perplexity from prior benchmark] |
| Equity price recovery | Trough-to-prior-peak: typically 2–4 years for genuine recoveries; some never recover to pre-distress highs |

**Overall average for surviving institutions:** ~18–24 months to operational stabilization; 3–5 years to full equity recovery. Failures resolve in weeks (binary) — no recovery.

## 3. 20-Year Survivorship Rate

No single published study gives a clean distress-cohort survivorship rate, but triangulating:
- FDIC Community Banking Study (1984–2011): only ~20% of sub-$100M banks survived the full 27-year horizon — but most exits were mergers, not failures [FDIC 2012]
- For banks that entered "problem bank list" (CAMELS 4-5), roughly 30–40% eventually failed vs. 60–70% that were acquired or recovered
- For the specific sub-population of distressed-but-surviving regionals (not on FDIC failed list), the 20-year survival rate is approximately **70–80%**, higher for larger/better-capitalized institutions

**Survivorship is strongly size-dependent:** Banks >$10bn assets — failure rate <2% over any 20yr horizon. Banks $1–10bn — failure rate ~5–10% in crisis periods. Banks <$1bn — failure rate 15–25% in peak crisis windows.

## 4. Turnaround Case Studies

### Case 1: Synovus Financial (SNV) — Genuine Deep Recovery ✓

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Period** | 2008–2013 |
| **Trigger** | Heavy CRE/ADC concentration in Georgia/Southeast; portfolio nearly 400% of Tier 1; housing crash wiped book value |
| **Trough** | Stock: $0.50 (2009), down from $30+ (2006) — ~98% drawdown |
| **Actions taken** | 1. TARP capital injection $968M (2008); 2. Massive loan workout program — sold $1.5bn+ distressed assets; 3. Management overhaul (new CEO 2010); 4. Divested non-core units (Total System Services stake); 5. Recapitalized via equity offering 2012 |
| **Outcome** | **Success** — returned to profitability 2012; repaid TARP 2013; rebuilt to investment grade |
| **Timeline** | 5 years trough to full operational recovery |
| **Stock recovery** | $0.50 trough → $12–14 by 2014 (~25x from trough, ~50% of pre-crisis peak) |
| **Pattern** | Gradual CRE stress + aggressive balance sheet surgery + capital raise — textbook regional bank recovery |

---

### Case 2: Regions Financial (RF) — TARP-Assisted Recovery ✓

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Period** | 2008–2012 |
| **Trigger** | Acquired AmSouth 2006 at peak; combined CRE + residential mortgage exposure; GFC wiped credit quality |
| **Trough** | Stock: $2.50 (2009), down from $15+ (2007) — ~83% drawdown |
| **Actions taken** | 1. TARP $3.5bn (2008); 2. Cost reduction — branch closures, headcount -15%; 3. Shifted loan mix away from CRE toward C&I; 4. New CFO + risk management overhaul; 5. Repaid TARP $3.5bn via public offering 2012 |
| **Outcome** | **Success** — operating above hurdle rates by 2013; TARP fully repaid |
| **Timeline** | ~4 years to operational normalization |
| **Stock recovery** | $2.50 trough → $11–12 by 2014 (~4.5x from trough) |
| **Pattern** | Loan mix shift + TARP stabilization + operational efficiency = effective playbook for cycle-driven distress |

### Case 3: New York Community Bancorp (NYCB) — Ongoing Partial Recovery ⚠

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Period** | 2024–present |
| **Trigger** | Acquired Flagstar Bank (2022) + Signature Bank deposits (2023); sudden size reclassification to "Category IV" requiring higher capital; Q4 2023 earnings shock — surprise $552M provision, dividend cut 70% |
| **Trough** | Stock: ~$2.80 (March 2024), down from $10+ (Jan 2024) — ~72% in 6 weeks |
| **Actions taken** | 1. Emergency $1.05bn capital raise led by Steven Mnuchin's Liberty Strategic Capital (March 2024); 2. New CEO Joseph Otting (former OCC Comptroller); 3. Portfolio review — 75% of CRE reviewed by mid-2024; 4. Reduced office CRE from $4.2bn → $3.3bn; 5. Exited non-core businesses [5] |
| **Outcome** | **Partial recovery in progress** — stabilized at $12–14 range by late 2024; return to profitability expected 2025 |
| **Timeline** | 12–18 months to stabilization; full recovery 2–3 years |
| **Stock recovery** | $2.80 trough → ~$12 (2024–25) — ~4x from trough but still well below pre-distress $10 Jan 2024 level adjusted for dilution |
| **Pattern** | Classic "size reclassification shock" — regulatory capital requirement step-change caused by acquisition. Emergency capital + credible management change = stabilization. Most investable of the three 2023-wave adjacent cases. |

### Case 4: SunTrust Banks (STI) — Full Recovery then Strategic Merger ✓

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Period** | 2008–2012 |
| **Trigger** | Residential mortgage exposure + Coke stock concentration (strategic holding depressed capital optionality); GFC credit losses |
| **Trough** | Stock: ~$13 (2011), down from $75+ (2007) — ~83% drawdown |
| **Actions taken** | 1. TARP $4.9bn (2008) — repaid 2011; 2. Sold Coke stake to shore up capital; 3. Mortgage servicing rights workout; 4. Expense reduction program; 5. Rebuilt capital ratios above "well-capitalized" by 2012 |
| **Outcome** | **Full recovery** — merged with BB&T to form Truist Financial (2019) at near-full valuation |
| **Timeline** | ~4 years to full capital recovery |
| **Stock recovery** | $13 trough → $60+ by 2014–15 (~4.6x from trough, exceeding pre-crisis level on adjusted basis) |
| **Pattern** | Concentrated mortgage stress + strategic asset sale + TARP = recovery. Merger exit rather than standalone recovery is common for fully-recovered mid-size regionals. |

### Case 5: First City Bancorporation (TX) — Failed Despite Assistance ✗

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Period** | 1987–1992 |
| **Trigger** | Texas oil bust + CRE collapse; capital ratio fell to -0.91% by March 1987 |
| **Actions taken** | 1. FDIC open-bank assistance 1987; 2. Outside capital injection approved 1988; 3. $926M FDIC assistance 1990 |
| **Outcome** | **Failure** — closed 1992 despite 5 years of support |
| **Timeline** | 5 years from initial shock to final closure |
| **Stock recovery** | $19.38 (1979) → $3.38 (1986) → effectively zero by 1992 |
| **Pattern** | Structural impairment from oil-patch CRE concentration in a mono-economy region. External capital injection alone cannot fix impaired asset base without addressable business. Key lesson: recovery requires a viable business to recover TO — not just capital. |

## 5. Synthesis: Recovery Pattern Typology

| Type | Conditions | Probability of recovery | Typical timeline | Stock outcome |
|------|-----------|------------------------|-----------------|---------------|
| **Cycle-driven distress, preserved franchise** | CRE/mortgage losses but diversified deposit base, no fraud, capital above 4% | ~75–85% | 3–5 years | 3–6x from trough, may not reach pre-crisis high |
| **Rate-shock + concentrated deposits** | Unrealized losses >50% CET1, uninsured >50% — but no contagion trigger yet | ~30–50% | 1–3 years or failure | Binary; either stabilizes fast or dies in days |
| **Acquisition-triggered reclassification** | Size jump forces higher capital requirements; no fundamental credit problem | ~70–80% | 12–24 months | 3–5x from trough with credible capital raise |
| **Oil/geography mono-economy CRE** | Loan book tied to single commodity or geography in secular decline | ~20–30% | 3–7 years or failure | Rarely exceeds 2x from trough; first opportunity to exit is often best |
| **Fraud-driven** | Internal diversion, restatement, OTS/OCC enforcement for concealment | ~5% | N/A | Goes to zero |

**Key pattern across successful turnarounds:** Cycle-driven distress + preserved franchise + management credibility + external capital (TARP or private) + loan workout program = recoverable. The franchise — branch network, customer relationships, deposit base — is the asset being preserved. When the franchise is intact, recovery is about time and capital. When it's impaired (fraud, concentrated mono-industry CRE), capital injections just delay the inevitable.
