## Historical Failure Patterns of U.S. Regional Banks (≈ 1988-2026)

|---|--------------|----------------|------------------------|------------------------|---------------|--------------------------------------|--------------------------------|
| 1 | **Washington Mutual (WaMu)** - Seattle, WA | 2008 | Sold to JPMorgan Chase in a closed-bank transaction; no loss to the Deposit Insurance Fund (DIF) | **Liquidity** (massive deposit run) + **Operational** (high-risk mortgage underwriting) | **Binary** - sudden run after rumors and Lehman collapse [6][8] | • Rapid growth in option-ARM & sub-prime loan portfolio (high charge-offs) [8]  <br>• CAMELS rating fell to “4-4-4-4-2” (poor asset quality & liquidity) [8]  <br>• $16.7 bn deposit outflows in 10 days (Sept 15-23) [6][9] | • Depositors fully protected; shareholders wiped out. <br>• Prompted tighter supervision of thrift-type lenders and highlighted need for liquidity buffers. |
| 2 | **IndyMac Bank** - Pasadena, CA | 2008 | Placed in conservatorship; assets later sold to OneWest Bank (later PNC) [15] | **Liquidity** (run on deposits) + **Regulatory** (OTS supervision gaps) | **Binary** - run triggered by public letter from Sen. Schumer [11][13] | • Heavy concentration in Alt-A & reverse mortgages; $10.7 bn of loans stuck on balance sheet [13]  <br>• CAMELS rating slipped below “well-capitalized” in early 2008 [11]  <br>• $1.55 bn deposit withdrawals (≈ 7.5 % of deposits) [13] | • Losses to DIF estimated at ≈ $9 bn (largest at the time) [11] <br>• Accelerated FDIC reforms for thrift supervision. |
| 3 | **Silicon Valley Bank (SVB)** - Santa Clara, CA | 2023 | FDIC receivership; assets transferred to a bridge bank then sold to First Citizens Bank [16][18] | **Liquidity** (run on uninsured deposits) + **Contagion** (spill-over from market panic) | **Binary** - deposit run after disclosure of $1.8 bn securities loss [18] | • CAMELS “satisfactory” but liquidity rating highest; however, 89 % of deposits were uninsured [20]  <br>• Large unrealised losses on long-duration securities as rates rose [18]  <br>• Social-media-driven “run” on March 9-10 [19] | • $42 bn of deposits withdrew in a day; $35 bn estimated cost to DIF [18]  <br>• Triggered systemic-risk exception, temporary guarantee for uninsured deposits, and new capital-adequacy proposals. |
| 4 | **Signature Bank** - New York, NY | 2023 | FDIC receivership; assets sold to Flagstar Bank (NYCB) [23][25] | **Liquidity** (over-reliance on uninsured crypto-related deposits) + **Regulatory** (weak risk-management) | **Binary** - sudden closure after SVB run amplified crypto-sector panic [23][25] | • Rapid growth into digital-asset clientele; > 70 % deposits uninsured [25]  <br>• Repeated FDIC supervisory letters flagging liquidity-risk weaknesses (2018-2022) [25]  <br>• Declining pledged-security reporting until day of failure [25] | • Estimated $2.5 bn loss to DIF [25]  <br>• Highlighted need for stronger oversight of crypto-exposed banks. |
| 5 | **First Republic Bank** - San Francisco, CA | 2023 | FDIC receivership; deposits & assets sold to JPMorgan Chase [31][33][34] | **Liquidity** (run on high-net-worth uninsured deposits) + **Contagion** (after SVB & Signature) | **Binary** - depositor flight following market panic [31][33] | • Heavy reliance on high-yield uninsured deposits (≈ 67 % uninsured) [31]  <br>• Large unrealised losses on long-duration loan portfolio; interest-rate risk not mitigated [31][33]  <br>• Stock price fell 90 % in weeks after SVB failure [31] | • $13-$15 bn estimated loss to DIF [31][33]  <br>• Prompted broader “bridge-bank” approach and discussions on deposit-insurance limits. |
| 6 | **Guaranty Bank (Texas)** - Austin, TX | 2009 | FDIC receivership; assets sold to BBVA USA [1] | **Operational** (poor loan underwriting & concentration) + **Regulatory** (failure to meet capital requirements) | **Gradual** - deteriorating asset quality over 2007-2009 [2] | • Asset quality decline; $13 bn loan losses on sub-prime & commercial real-estate [2]  <br>• CAMELS rating fell to “4-4-4-4-2” in 2009 [2] | • $13 bn loss to DIF; shareholders wiped out; accelerated FDIC focus on loan-quality monitoring. |
| 7 | **Colonial Bank** - Montgomery, AL | 2009 | FDIC receivership; assets sold to BB&T [2] | **Liquidity** (run on deposits) + **Regulatory** (insufficient capital buffers) | **Binary** - rapid deposit outflows after loss of confidence [2] | • Large exposure to real-estate loans that turned sour in the housing crash [2]  <br>• CAMELS rating dropped sharply in 2008-09 [2] | • $25 bn cost to DIF (largest at the time) [2]  <br>• Led to stricter capital-adequacy standards for midsize banks. |
| 8 | **Metropolitan Capital Bank & Trust** - Chicago, IL | 2026 (Jan 30) | FDIC receivership; deposits & assets assumed by First Independence Bank [36][39] | **Liquidity** (insufficient core deposits) + **Operational** (small-scale mis-management) | **Gradual** - declining deposit base and asset quality over 2025 [36] | • Asset-to-deposit ratio > 1.2; core deposits fell below 50 % of total [36]  <br>• CAMELS rating slipped to “4-3-4-3-2” in late-2025 [36] | • No systemic spill-over; depositors protected; serves as early-warning case for tightening credit conditions in 2026. |

\* **Failure-type**:  
- **Gradual** - problems built up over months/years, allowing regulators to observe deteriorating metrics before closure.  
- **Binary** - a sudden, market-driven run or regulatory action that precipitated immediate closure.

### Narrative Highlights

1. **Liquidity-driven collapses dominate** - All eight cases feature a liquidity shortfall, either from a rapid deposit run (WaMu, IndyMac, SVB, Signature, First Republic, Colonial, Metropolitan) or from chronic funding mismatches (Guaranty). The 2023 wave (SVB, Signature, First Republic) showed how a single shock can cascade through the system, creating **contagion** effects that turn otherwise “gradual” stress into binary failures.

2. **Operational weaknesses often underlie liquidity stress** - Washington Mutual and Guaranty Bank pursued aggressive, high-risk loan growth without adequate underwriting controls, eroding asset quality and capital buffers, which later manifested as liquidity crises.

3. **Regulatory oversight gaps** - IndyMac’s failure highlighted the inability of the Office of Thrift Supervision to act on emerging problems, while Signature Bank repeatedly ignored FDIC supervisory letters on liquidity risk. These cases illustrate the importance of timely, decisive regulator action.

4. **Fraud is rare among large regional failures** - None of the selected banks were primarily driven by fraud; the failures are better explained by credit-risk, liquidity, and governance issues. (The list of failed banks includes a few fraud-related closures, but they are outside the scope of this sample.)

5. **Early warning signals** - Consistent patterns emerge:
   - **CAMELS rating declines** (especially in asset quality and liquidity) months before closure.  
   - **Rapid growth of uninsured or brokered deposits** (> 60 % of total) that can evaporate under stress.  
   - **Large unrealised losses on long-duration securities** as interest rates rise (SVB, First Republic).  
   - **Public disclosures or political letters** that trigger panic (IndyMac).  
   - **Concentration in volatile sectors** (crypto for Signature; tech-venture for SVB).

6. **Post-failure outcomes** - In every case, FDIC ensured **full protection of insured deposits**; shareholders were eliminated, and unsecured creditors often recovered little. The DIF incurred losses ranging from a few hundred million (Metropolitan) to tens of billions (First Republic, Colonial). The systemic impact varied: the 2008 failures contributed to the broader financial crisis, while the 2023 failures prompted an unprecedented **systemic-risk exception** and temporary guarantee of uninsured deposits to halt contagion.

### Lessons for Stakeholders

| Stakeholder | Key Take-away |
|-------------|---------------|
| **Depositors** | Monitor the share of uninsured/brokered deposits; large concentrations signal heightened run risk. |
| **Shareholders** | Governance and risk-management failures can wipe out equity quickly; board oversight is critical. |
| **Counterparties** | Verify a bank’s pledged-security reporting; mis-reporting can lead to sudden liquidity shortfalls (Signature). |
| **Regulators** | Early CAMELS alerts and sector-specific exposure (e.g., crypto, tech) should trigger pre-emptive corrective actions. |
| **Policy Makers** | The 2023 crisis shows the need for flexible deposit-insurance tools and clearer liquidity-stress testing standards. |
