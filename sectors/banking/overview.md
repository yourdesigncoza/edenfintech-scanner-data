# Banking Sector — Overview

## Sector Map

| Sub-sector | FMP Industry | NYSE Count | Description |
|-----------|-------------|------------|-------------|
| **Diversified Banks** | Banks - Diversified | 20 | Large, geographically diverse institutions ($250B-$4T+ assets). G-SIBs and near-G-SIBs. Revenue: 50-65% NII, 35-50% fees/trading/advisory. Includes Big Four (JPM, BAC, C, WFC) plus investment bank-heavy (GS, MS). |
| **Regional Banks** | Banks - Regional | 79 | Mid-sized banks ($10B-$250B assets) serving defined geographic footprints. Revenue: 65-80% NII, 20-35% fees. Higher CRE concentration, simpler business models, less diversified funding. |

## Cross-Cutting Themes

### 1. Post-2023 Regulatory Regime Shift
The SVB/Signature/First Republic failures (March-May 2023) triggered the most significant regulatory recalibration since Dodd-Frank. Both sub-sectors face:
- Basel III Endgame reproposal (higher capital requirements, AOCI opt-out removal for $100B+ banks)
- Long-term debt requirements for regionals ($100B+)
- Enhanced liquidity scrutiny (deposit outflow assumptions revised upward)
- FDIC special assessments to refill the Deposit Insurance Fund

**Impact asymmetry:** Regionals bear disproportionate regulatory burden increase relative to their scale. G-SIBs already operate under Category I/II requirements; Category IV banks ($100B-$250B) face the steepest adjustment.

### 2. CRE Concentration Risk
Regional banks hold ~70% of US CRE loans despite ~30% of total banking assets. Office vacancy rates remain elevated post-COVID. This is a structural overhang that:
- Creates a slow-motion credit quality deterioration (not a single event)
- Disproportionately affects regionals vs diversified banks
- Has already triggered regulatory action (NYCB 2024)
- Will likely intensify through 2025-2027 as office loans mature and require refinancing at higher rates

### 3. Digital Banking and Deposit Franchise Erosion
The 2023 crisis demonstrated that deposit "stickiness" — a core assumption of bank valuation — is unreliable in the digital age:
- SVB lost $42B in one day via electronic transfers
- Social media (Twitter, VC group chats) accelerated panic faster than any historical precedent
- Uninsured deposits are now recognized as "hot money" by regulators
- Both sub-sectors must hold more HQLA (High-Quality Liquid Assets) against revised outflow assumptions

### 4. Interest Rate Sensitivity
Both sub-sectors are NII-dependent, but differently:
- **Diversified banks** can offset NIM compression with trading revenue (rate volatility benefits CIB desks)
- **Regional banks** are pure spread businesses — NIM compression hits revenue directly with limited offset
- Yield curve shape matters more than absolute rate level for both

### 5. Consolidation Wave
Banking consolidation continues: ~9,000 FDIC-insured institutions in 2004, <4,600 today. Drivers:
- Regulatory compliance costs favor scale
- Technology investment is table-stakes (expensive for small banks)
- Distressed regionals are acquisition fodder for super-regionals
- M&A provides both a turnaround exit path and a valuation floor for well-run regional franchises

## Inter-Sub-Sector Dynamics

1. **Competition for deposits:** Diversified banks (with "Too Big To Fail" implicit guarantee) gained deposit share during and after the 2023 crisis at regionals' expense. JPM alone received ~$50B in new deposits during March 2023.

2. **M&A food chain:** Diversified banks acquire distressed regionals (JPM acquired First Republic, PNC acquired National City, WFC acquired Wachovia). Strong regionals acquire weaker regionals (Banc of California acquired PacWest). This creates a natural flow from distressed to strong.

3. **Regulatory arbitrage:** Pre-2023, Category IV banks ($100B-$250B) had lighter regulation than Category I-III. Post-2023, this gap is narrowing, reducing the "regulatory discount" that benefited large regionals.

4. **CRE risk concentration:** Diversified banks can absorb CRE losses across their global revenue streams. For regionals, CRE losses hit concentrated loan books with no diversification buffer. A CRE downturn that is manageable for JPM could be existential for a regional with CRE >300% of capital.

## EdenFinTech Turnaround Lens

**Key challenge for the scanner:** Banking turnarounds have historically delivered 15-25% CAGR over extended periods (5-15 years), not the explosive 30%+ CAGR the strategy requires. The exceptions are trough-entry plays (SNV: $1 to $54 = 4,000%, but over 7 years) where timing the absolute bottom is critical.

**Where opportunities might exist:**
- Post-crisis contagion victims that were fundamentally sound but dragged down by association (the 2023 "guilt by association" pattern)
- Regionals with strong deposit franchises in attractive geographies trading below TBV due to temporary credit cycle stress
- Banks emerging from consent orders where regulatory resolution creates a discrete re-rating catalyst

**Where the strategy likely fails:**
- Banks with structural CRE problems (slow-motion, not catalyst-driven)
- Banks requiring regulatory approval that operates on discretionary timelines
- Banks where the "turnaround" is really just waiting for the rate cycle to normalize (no management action, no catalysts)
