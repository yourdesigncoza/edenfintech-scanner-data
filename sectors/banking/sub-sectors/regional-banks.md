# Regional Banks

## Overview

Regional banks are US-based banking institutions typically holding $10B-$250B in total assets, serving defined geographic footprints ranging from a few states to multi-state regions. Unlike diversified/money center banks (JPM, BAC, C, WFC), regionals derive 65-80% of revenue from net interest income (NII), with limited investment banking, trading, or global operations. Fee income sources include treasury management, wealth management, mortgage origination/servicing, and commercial card programs — but these rarely exceed 25-35% of total revenue.

The 79 NYSE-listed regional banks span a wide range: from large regionals approaching G-SIB scale (USB at ~$680B, PNC at ~$560B) down to community-oriented banks with $10-20B in assets. The defining characteristic is geographic and loan-book concentration — regionals are disproportionately exposed to their home markets' economic health, particularly commercial real estate (CRE). As of 2024, regional banks hold approximately 70% of all US CRE loans, despite representing roughly 30% of total banking assets. This concentration is both their competitive advantage (deep local relationships, superior underwriting knowledge) and their existential risk.

Capital intensity mirrors diversified banks: 10-12x leverage, ROA of 0.8-1.3% translating to ROE of 8-14% through leverage. However, regionals operate with thinner capital buffers above regulatory minimums and less diversified funding sources. The 2023 crisis demonstrated that regional bank deposit franchises — once considered "sticky" — can evaporate in hours when uninsured deposit concentrations are high and social media accelerates panic.

## Key Metrics & KPIs

| Metric | What It Measures | Healthy Range | Red Flag |
|--------|-----------------|---------------|----------|
| CET1 Ratio | Highest-quality capital vs risk-weighted assets | >10% (min 4.5% + buffers) | <8% or declining trend |
| Tangible Book Value per Share | Net tangible assets per share | Growing 5-8% annually | Declining or stagnant |
| Return on Tangible Common Equity (ROTCE) | Profitability relative to tangible equity | 12-16% (excellent >16%) | <10% sustained (below cost of equity) |
| Return on Assets (ROA) | Profitability relative to total assets | 0.9-1.3% (excellent >1.2%) | <0.7% |
| Net Interest Margin (NIM) | Spread between lending and funding rates | 2.8-3.5% for regionals | <2.5% or compressing >50bps YoY |
| Efficiency Ratio | Noninterest expense / total revenue | 52-62% (lower = better) | >65% sustained |
| NPL Ratio | Non-performing loans / total loans | <1.5% | >3% or rising rapidly |
| Net Charge-Off Ratio | Actual loan losses / average loans | 0.2-0.6% through-cycle | >1.0% or spiking |
| Texas Ratio | (NPLs + REO) / (tangible equity + loan loss reserves) | <25% | >50% (>100% = failure risk) |
| Loan-to-Deposit Ratio | Total loans / total deposits | 75-90% | >100% (funding stress) or <65% (underearning) |
| CRE Concentration | CRE loans / total risk-based capital | <200% | >300% (regulatory trigger) |
| Construction Concentration | Construction loans / total risk-based capital | <50% | >100% (regulatory trigger) |
| Uninsured Deposit Ratio | Uninsured deposits / total deposits | <40% | >50% (run risk, see SVB at 94%) |
| Non-Interest Income Mix | Fee income as % of total revenue | 20-35% | <15% (highly NII-dependent, rate-sensitive) |
| Cost of Deposits | Average interest paid on deposits | 1.5-2.5% (rate dependent) | Rising faster than asset yields |

## Valuation Approach

- **Primary method:** Price-to-Tangible Book Value (P/TBV), regressed against forward Return on Tangible Common Equity (ROTCE). A regional bank earning its cost of equity (~10-11%) trades at ~1.0x TBV; each 1% ROTCE above COE adds roughly 0.10-0.15x.
- **Secondary method:** Price-to-Earnings (P/E) on normalized earnings. Use 8-10x for banks earning at/below cost of equity, 10-13x for banks earning above COE sustainably.
- **Why NOT FCF multiples:** Same as diversified banks — bank "free cash flow" is meaningless. Lending IS the business. Capital adequacy requirements constrain distributions. Use earnings-based or book-value-based approaches exclusively.
- **Typical multiple range:** 0.5-1.8x TBV. Strong performers (FITB, MTB) at 1.2-1.8x; distressed or sub-COE earners at 0.5-0.8x. M&A premium: 1.5-2.0x TBV for well-run franchises.
- **Dividend Discount Model (DDM):** Appropriate for mature regionals with stable payout policies (25-40% payout ratio). Discount projected dividends at 10-12% COE.
- **M&A as valuation floor:** Regionals with strong deposit franchises in attractive geographies rarely trade below 1.0x TBV for extended periods — acquirers step in. This provides a soft floor absent existential risk.
- **Common pitfalls:** (1) Using P/E without normalizing for credit cycle — provisions swing earnings 30-50% in downturns. (2) Ignoring unrealized HTM losses that reduce economic TBV below reported TBV (the SVB killer). (3) Comparing P/TBV across regionals without adjusting for ROTCE differences and CRE concentration. (4) Treating deposit costs as stable when rate environment is shifting. (5) Ignoring M&A premium/discount — regionals in consolidation-target geographies deserve different multiples than those in saturated markets.

**Patterns:**
- Regional bank turnarounds take 3-7 years for balance sheet repair, with stock recovery often leading fundamental recovery by 12-18 months
- Successful turnarounds require: (1) decisive asset disposition (selling bad loans at a loss rather than slow-walking), (2) capital raise early (dilutive but survivable), (3) simplification of structure/operations
- Independent survivorship rate for distressed regionals is low (<40% over 20 years) — M&A is the most common exit path, either as "take-under" (failure prevention) or "scale play" (post-recovery merger)
- The long timelines (3-7 years) challenge the EdenFinTech 30% CAGR hurdle unless entry is near the absolute trough

## Risk Profile

- **Default risk type:** Regulatory / Concentration
- **Cyclicality:** High — NII moves with rates, credit losses spike in recessions, CRE values are cyclical
- **Regulatory discretion:** High — Fed, OCC, FDIC, state regulators all have discretionary authority. Category IV banks ($100B-$250B) face increasing regulatory burden post-2023
- **Macro sensitivity:**
  - Interest rates (short-term): HIGH — directly drives deposit costs and floating-rate loan yields
  - Interest rates (long-term) / yield curve shape: HIGH — NIM is a spread business; flat/inverted curve compresses margins
  - Credit cycle (consumer): MODERATE — regionals have consumer exposure but less than money center banks
  - Credit cycle (CRE): EXTREME — regionals hold ~70% of US CRE loans. Office vacancy + refinancing at higher rates is an existential sector risk
  - Unemployment: HIGH — drives consumer and SME default rates, particularly in concentrated geographic footprints
  - Liquidity conditions: HIGH — deposit flight risk demonstrated in 2023. Digital banking makes runs faster
  - Inflation: MODERATE — indirect via rates and credit quality
  - Commodity prices: MODERATE (for ag/energy-belt regionals like CMA, BOK, ZION) — affects borrower credit quality in resource-dependent regions
  - Commercial real estate cycles: EXTREME — the dominant risk factor for most regionals
  - Small business formation/failure: MODERATE — regionals are primary SME lenders in their footprints
- **Binary trigger frequency:** Occasional but accelerating in the digital age

### Kill Factors (hard reject — maps to enrichment override triggers)

1. **CET1 below regulatory minimum / PCA territory** — Bank faces mandatory FDIC seizure. No turnaround path. Example: IndyMac (2008), capital ratios collapsed, seized on a Friday.

2. **Active deposit run or liquidity crisis** — Once depositors flee, it becomes a death spiral in the digital age. Example: SVB lost $42B in one day (March 2023); First Republic lost $100B+ in Q1 2023 despite $30B rescue injection.

3. **CRE concentration >300% of capital WITH rising NPLs** — Regulatory MRIA forces loan sales or dilutive equity raises, triggering confidence spiral. Example: NYCB (2024) required emergency $1B equity raise from vulture capital.

4. **Fraud or material financial misstatement** — Destroys depositor and investor trust. Example: Colonial BancGroup (2009) — $2.9B in fake assets with Taylor, Bean & Whitaker. Heartland Tri-State (2023) — CEO embezzled $47M into crypto scam.

5. **Uninsured deposit concentration >75% without offsetting liquidity** — Creates structural vulnerability to instantaneous runs. Example: SVB at 94% uninsured, Signature Bank high uninsured + crypto concentration.

6. **Negative tangible book value** — For a bank, this means insolvency. No turnaround path without massive dilutive capital raise or FDIC intervention.

### Friction Factors (PCS modifiers — reduce confidence, don't kill thesis)

1. **CRE concentration 200-300% of capital** — suggested friction: -1, rationale: elevated concentration risk but below regulatory trigger. Requires careful monitoring of office/multifamily exposure mix.

2. **Uninsured deposits 40-75%** — suggested friction: -1, rationale: elevated run risk but manageable with strong liquidity buffers and transparent communication. Western Alliance survived 2023 at ~50% uninsured by aggressively disclosing insured deposit data.

3. **Earnings below cost of equity for 3+ years** — suggested friction: -1, rationale: market will not re-rate P/TBV until ROTCE sustainably exceeds COE. Extended timeline for turnaround.

4. **Unrealized securities losses >20% of tangible equity** — suggested friction: -1, rationale: economic TBV is materially lower than reported. Risk of forced realization if deposits flee (the SVB mechanism).

5. **Geographic concentration in economically stressed region** — suggested friction: -1, rationale: regional banks cannot diversify away from their footprint quickly. Recession in home market directly impairs loan book.

6. **Pending regulatory changes (Basel III Endgame, LTD requirements)** — suggested friction: -1, rationale: book values are subject to regulatory debasement. Rules regarding leverage being rewritten in real-time for $100B+ banks.

7. **Recent M&A integration (within 2 years)** — suggested friction: -1, rationale: integration risk adds operational uncertainty to turnaround thesis. Example: NYCB's acquisition of Signature Bank assets preceded its 2024 crisis.

## Thesis-Breaking Patterns

- **CRE concentration blow-up:** Bank holds disproportionate CRE exposure. Vacancies rise + refinancing at higher rates causes LTV inversion. Reserves fail to cover charge-offs. Gradual then sudden. Example: Bank of New England (1991), NYCB (2024).

- **Digital deposit run / contagion:** Social media amplifies concerns, digital banking enables instant withdrawal. Bank may be fundamentally solvent but destroyed by speed of flight. Binary. Example: SVB (2023) — $42B in one day. First Republic (2023) — survived weeks but profitability was destroyed.

- **Interest rate mismatch (duration trap):** Bank invests in long-duration bonds/mortgages at low rates. Rates rise, bonds lose market value. Forced to sell at losses or face economic insolvency. Gradual (unrealized) then binary (if deposits flee). Example: SVB (2023), First Republic (2023).

- **Credit cycle timing trap:** Bank appears cheap at cycle peak when provisions are low and earnings are high. True cost becomes apparent in downturn. Gradual. Example: National City Corp (2007-2008), many regionals in 2006-2009.

- **Regulatory escalation spiral:** Initial concern leads to consent order, which impairs earnings, which attracts more scrutiny, which leads to more restrictive orders. Gradual. Example: Wells Fargo (2016-2025 at diversified scale), NYCB (2024 at regional scale).

- **Geographic economic shock:** Single-market dependency means local economic distress directly hits loan book. No diversification buffer. Gradual. Example: Comerica during Texas energy crisis (2015-2016), Oklahoma/energy-belt banks in oil downturns.

## Evidence Requirements

### Must-Have Data Points

| Data Point | Where to Find It | Why It Matters |
|-----------|-----------------|----------------|
| CET1 Ratio (current + trend) | 10-K Item 8 (Notes), 10-Q, FMP ratios endpoint | Capital adequacy is existential for banks |
| Tangible Book Value per Share | 10-K Item 8, FMP metrics (tangibleBookValuePerShare) | Primary valuation anchor for regionals |
| ROTCE (Return on Tangible Common Equity) | Calculate from 10-K; net income / avg tangible common equity | Determines justified P/TBV multiple |
| Net Interest Margin | 10-K Item 7 (MD&A), FMP metrics endpoint | Revenue quality and rate sensitivity |
| Efficiency Ratio | 10-K Item 7, calculate from FMP income statement | Operating leverage and cost discipline |
| CRE Concentration (% of capital) | 10-K Item 7 or Item 8, FDIC Call Reports (FFIEC CDR) | THE key risk metric for regionals. >300% triggers regulatory action |
| CRE Loan Composition (office vs multifamily vs retail vs industrial) | 10-K Item 7 supplemental tables | Office exposure is the current hot-button risk |
| NPL Ratio and NCO Rate | 10-K Item 8, FDIC Call Reports | Credit quality — the key earnings driver |
| Provision for Credit Losses (quarterly trend) | 10-Q, FMP income statement | Leading indicator of management's credit outlook |
| Unrealized gains/losses on securities (AFS + HTM) | 10-K Item 8 Note on investments, OCI statement | Hidden balance sheet risk (SVB's killer) |
| Deposit Composition (insured vs uninsured) | 10-K Item 8, FDIC Call Reports, earnings call disclosures | Liquidity risk — uninsured deposits are flight risk |
| Deposit Cost Trend | 10-K/Q MD&A, FMP metrics | Rising deposit costs compress NIM and signal franchise weakness |
| Loan-to-Deposit Ratio | Calculate from 10-K | Funding stability indicator |
| Stress Test Results (if applicable) | Federal Reserve website (annual, June) | Regulatory view of downside risk (Cat III/IV banks) |
| Consent Orders / Enforcement Actions | Fed, OCC, FDIC enforcement action databases | Regulatory overhang assessment |

### Red Flags If Missing

- If CRE concentration data is not disclosed by loan type (office/multifamily/retail), the primary risk factor is unquantifiable
- If uninsured deposit ratio is not available, the 2023-style run risk cannot be assessed
- If unrealized securities losses are not broken out (AFS vs HTM), the SVB-style risk is hidden
- If ROTCE or TBV per share is not calculable, valuation is impossible

## Epistemic Profile

| PCS Question | Typical Answer | Rationale |
|-------------|---------------|-----------|
| Q1: Operational risk? | Partial Yes | Core risks (credit, interest rate) are modelable with standard tools. However, CRE concentration risk and deposit behavior in a social-media panic are harder to model. Simpler business models than G-SIBs make fundamentals more transparent. |
| Q2: Regulatory minimal? | No | High regulatory discretion — Fed, OCC, FDIC all have authority. Category IV banks face increasing burden post-2023. Systemic Risk Exception decisions are political and unpredictable. |
| Q3: Historical precedent? | Yes (moderate) | Strong turnaround precedent base: RF, SNV, ZION, CFG, CMA all recovered from severe stress. But independent survivorship rate is low (<40%) — most distressed regionals get acquired rather than independently recovering. |
| Q4: Non-binary outcome? | No | Regional banking outcomes are frequently binary due to FDIC receivership mechanism. A bank is either open or seized on a Friday night. The 2023 crisis showed that the transition from "stressed" to "seized" can happen in 48 hours. Gradient outcomes exist (slow NIM recovery, gradual credit normalization) but the tail risk is always binary. |
| Q5: Macro/geo limited? | Partial Yes | Almost entirely domestic (no FX, limited geopolitical). But heavily exposed to US interest rates, credit cycles, and regional economic conditions. More macro-sensitive than typical "domestic" companies due to leverage. |

**Epistemic stability:** 2/5 — Regional bank fundamentals are transparent and well-documented (simpler balance sheets than G-SIBs), but the 2023 crisis proved that deposit stickiness is no longer reliable in the digital age. A bank run that took weeks now takes hours. CRE concentration adds a structural overhang that is difficult to time. The combination of transparent fundamentals + binary tail risk + digital run speed creates a paradox: you can model the balance sheet precisely but cannot predict whether a confidence crisis will destroy it overnight.

**Suggested PCS friction:** -1 — Regulatory risk and binary deposit-run risk dominate. The CRE concentration overhang adds structural uncertainty. Apply -1 as default; increase to -2 if the specific bank has CRE >250% of capital, uninsured deposits >50%, or unrealized losses >15% of tangible equity.
