## Cross-Sector Patterns

### What Successful Turnarounds Have in Common

1. **Portfolio focus over broad restructuring.** ConAgra, Constellation, and Church & Dwight all succeeded by narrowing focus -- divesting weak brands/categories and doubling down on defensible positions. Trying to fix everything simultaneously (Kraft Heinz, Edgewell) produces slow, inconclusive results.

2. **Brand investment over cost-cutting.** Every sustained recovery involved maintaining or increasing brand investment despite short-term pressure. The 3G Capital cost-cutting model (Kraft Heinz) demonstrably failed in consumer staples -- the sector requires ongoing brand investment to maintain shelf space and consumer preference.

3. **Catching a consumer trend.** Boston Beer's partial recovery came from Twisted Tea catching the FMB trend. ConAgra's recovery aligned with frozen food category revitalization. Companies that wait for "market conditions to improve" without driving innovation do not recover.

4. **Balance sheet capacity to invest.** Overleveraged companies (TreeHouse, Revlon, Dean Foods) cannot fund turnarounds because debt service crowds out brand investment and innovation spending.

### Common Recovery Timelines

| Scenario | Typical Timeline | Examples |
|----------|-----------------|----------|
| Portfolio rationalization (divest + focus) | 3-5 years | ConAgra (4 years), Constellation wine exit (4+ years) |
| Brand revitalization | 2-3 years for initial results; 5+ for full recovery | KHC (5+ years, still in progress); SAM (2 years partial) |
| Bankruptcy + relaunch | 1-3 years to emerge; 5-10 years to full value creation | Hostess (10 years from bankruptcy to $5.6B sale) |
| Cost restructuring only | 1-2 years for margin improvement; then volume resumes declining | AB InBev (temporary margin uplift, structural volume decline continues) |

### Failure Modes by Frequency

| Failure Mode | Frequency in Sample | Binary or Gradual | Outcome |
|-------------|--------------------|--------------------|---------|
| Secular demand decline (milk, wine, mainstream beer) | Most common | Gradual | Slow erosion; eventual liquidation or absorption (Dean Foods, Treasury Wine) |
| Overleveraged acquisition | Common | Binary-ish | Debt spiral prevents reinvestment (TreeHouse, Revlon) |
| Brand underinvestment / cost-cutting trap | Common | Gradual | Share loss compounds until irreversible (Kraft Heinz early years, Avon) |
| Channel disruption (DTC razors, Walmart self-sourcing) | Occasional | Binary-ish | Structural revenue loss with no recovery path (Dean Foods / Walmart) |
| Product contamination / recall | Occasional | Binary | $50M-$500M+ cost; survivable for large companies, potentially fatal for small ones |

## Probability Calibration Guidance for Analysts

When analyzing a Consumer Defensive turnaround candidate:

1. **Start with sub-sector base rate** (see tables above)
2. **If no sector knowledge or thin precedent:** default to 50% band
3. **Name the closest historical precedent** and explain similarity/differences
4. **Apply Likert adjustments:**
   - Management execution: +10% / 0% / -10%
   - Balance sheet survival: +10% / 0% / -10%
   - Market conditions: +10% / 0% / -10%
5. **Round to nearest band (50/60/70/80)**
6. **Check probability ceilings:** revenue/volume declined 3+ years -> max 65%
