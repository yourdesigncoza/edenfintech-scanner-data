## Cross-Cutting Themes

### 1. Private Label as Structural Competitive Threat
Private label reached record 20.7% of US retail spending in 2024 (+3.9% YoY vs 1% for national brands). Kirkland Signature generates $74.6B (30% of Costco revenue). Over 80% of consumers now rate PL quality equal or better than national brands. This is structural, not cyclical -- it affects packaged foods and household products most directly, but is also emerging in beverages.

### 2. Premiumization as the Only Viable Growth Strategy
Across all four sub-sectors, the consistent pattern is: value/mainstream segments are declining while premium segments grow. Craft beer losing share but premium beer growing. Wine consumption declining but premium wine holds. Packaged food faces PL pressure at value tier. HPC premium segment growing at 7.4% CAGR. Companies that cannot credibly reposition to premium are in structural decline.

### 3. GLP-1 Weight-Loss Drugs as Novel Demand Risk
GLP-1 drugs (Ozempic, Wegovy, Mounjaro) represent an unprecedented potential demand disruptor for food and beverage consumption. No historical analog exists. Impact magnitude is unknown but the market is pricing in risk, particularly for snacking/processed food and alcoholic beverage categories. Consumer staples stocks have underperformed partly due to this overhang.

### 4. Volume Decline Masked by Price Increases
A recurring pattern across sub-sectors: companies report flat or growing revenue driven by pricing actions, while underlying volume declines. This creates a misleading picture of business health. Analysts must decompose revenue into volume vs price/mix in every sub-sector.

### 5. Under-Investment Trap (3G Capital Playbook)
The most common thesis-breaking pattern in consumer staples: cost-cutting to boost near-term margins at the expense of brand investment, R&D, and innovation. Eventually brand equity atrophies and market share declines become irreversible. Kraft Heinz is the cautionary tale; ConAgra's recovery required reversing this approach.

## Macro Context

Consumer staples stocks have underperformed the S&P 500 in 2023-2025, driven by investor preference for AI/growth stocks and sector-specific headwinds (GLP-1 concerns, PL competition, volume softness). Consumer spending is expected to slow to 3.7% nominal growth in 2025 and 2.9% in 2026 (vs 5.7% in 2024) due to labor market cooling, tariff-induced inflation, and policy uncertainty ([Morgan Stanley](https://www.morganstanley.com/insights/articles/us-consumer-spending-trends-2025)).

However, consumer staples remain defensive in recessions -- food and household products are non-discretionary. The sector should benefit relatively if the economy weakens, as capital rotates from growth to defensive names. Interest rate cuts could also support the sector's income/dividend appeal.

Tariff risk is moderate: mostly domestic supply chains but some imported inputs (aluminum for beverage cans, agricultural commodities, glass packaging). Alcoholic beverages face the most tariff exposure (Mexican agave, EU retaliatory tariffs on bourbon).

## Inter-Sub-Sector Dynamics

**Beverages vs Packaged Foods:** Compete for share of stomach and consumer spending. RTD cocktails and functional beverages are cannibalizing both traditional beer and some packaged snack occasions. The category boundary is blurring (e.g., energy drinks, protein shakes).

**Alcoholic Beverages vs Non-Alcoholic Alternatives:** NA beer sales up 111% by volume 2021-2025. Spirits companies expanding into NA categories. This is both a threat (volume cannibalization) and an opportunity (margin expansion if brands can capture NA occasions).

**Household Products vs Personal Care:** Different competitive dynamics despite sharing FMP industry classification. Household products face intense PL competition (commoditized categories). Personal care has more brand loyalty and premium positioning potential. When analyzing companies in this FMP industry, always identify which segment dominates revenue.

**Private Label Manufacturers vs Brand Owners:** TreeHouse Foods (PL manufacturer, taken private 2025 at $2.9B) represents the opposite end of the value chain from branded players. PL manufacturers have structurally lower margins but benefit from the same secular PL share gains that threaten branded companies. Different thesis, different valuation framework.
