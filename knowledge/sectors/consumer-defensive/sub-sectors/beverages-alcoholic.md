# Beverages - Alcoholic (Brewers)

## Overview

The US beer market was valued at approximately $107 billion in 2024, with the broader North American beer market projected to reach $225 billion by 2030 at a 5.2% CAGR ([Grand View Research](https://www.grandviewresearch.com/industry-analysis/beer-market)). The industry is dominated by macro brewers (67.2% revenue share) with craft brewers holding 13.3% volume share ([Brewers Association](https://www.brewersassociation.org/association-news/brewers-association-reports-2024-u-s-craft-brewing-industry-figures/)).

Revenue models center on production and distribution of beer through a three-tier system (brewer-distributor-retailer). Capital intensity is moderate-to-high due to brewing facilities, cold-chain logistics, and brand investment. The industry is experiencing secular volume decline -- the overall US beer market declined 1.2% in volume in 2024 -- offset partially by premiumization and price/mix improvements. Since 2013, the top two traditional brewers have lost approximately 16 share points to craft, imports, and adjacent categories ([NBWA](https://nbwa.org/resources/fast-facts/)).

Key trends reshaping the competitive landscape include the rise of non-alcoholic beer (NA beer sales up 111% by volume 2021-2025), ready-to-drink cocktails cannibalizing beer occasions, and GLP-1 weight-loss drugs potentially reducing alcohol consumption.

## Key Metrics & KPIs

| Metric | What It Measures | Healthy Range | Red Flag |
|--------|-----------------|---------------|----------|
| Volume growth (%) | Organic demand trajectory | Flat to +2% | Declining 3+ consecutive years |
| Revenue per hectoliter | Pricing power / mix improvement | Rising YoY | Declining while volumes also decline |
| Gross margin (%) | Production efficiency + pricing | 50-60% (large-cap) | Below 45% or declining 3+ years |
| EBITDA margin (%) | Operating profitability | 25-35% (large-cap) | Below 20% |
| FCF conversion (%) | Cash generation quality | 70-90% of EBITDA | Below 60% |
| Net debt / EBITDA | Leverage | 2.0-3.5x | Above 4.5x |
| Market share (%) | Competitive position | Stable or growing | Losing 1+ pts/year |
| Distribution points | Route-to-market reach | Growing or stable | Losing shelf space / tap handles |
| Brand investment / revenue | Marketing spend adequacy | 8-12% | Below 6% (under-investing) |
| Days sales outstanding | Distributor relationship health | 30-45 days | Above 60 days |
| CapEx / revenue | Maintenance + growth investment | 5-10% | Above 15% without growth payoff |
| Revenue mix (premium %) | Portfolio quality | Increasing | Declining / dependent on economy brands |
| SG&A / revenue | Overhead efficiency | 15-25% | Above 30% |
| Return on invested capital | Capital allocation quality | 10-15%+ | Below 8% or declining trend |

## Valuation Approach

- **Primary method:** EV/EBITDA -- standard for beverage companies given varying capital structures and tax profiles
- **Secondary method:** P/FCF, P/E for companies with stable capital structures
- **Why NOT pure FCF multiples:** Large brewers carry significant goodwill from M&A (e.g., Molson Coors $3.65B impairment Q3 2025); FCF can be distorted by working capital swings in seasonal business
- **Typical multiple range:** 10-14x EV/EBITDA for large-cap brewers; 8-11x for declining volume profiles; 14-18x for premium/growth-oriented portfolios
- **Common pitfalls:** (1) Ignoring volume decline trajectory -- a brewer losing 4-6% volume annually trades at massive discount to one growing 1-2%; (2) Overpaying for "premiumization narrative" without volume proof; (3) Constellation Brands trades at premium due to beer import growth + Modelo franchise, not comparable to domestic-only brewers

**Discount schedule from baseline (12-14x):**
- Declining volume 2+ years: -2x to -3x
- Above-average leverage: -1x to -2x
- Secular category headwind (beer losing to spirits/RTD): -2x to -3x
- Limited pricing power (economy segment): -1x to -2x

## Turnaround Precedents

| Company | Period | Situation | Actions Taken | Outcome | Timeline | Stock Recovery |
|---------|--------|-----------|--------------|---------|----------|----------------|
| Molson Coors (TAP) | 2025-ongoing | $3.65B goodwill impairment, 6% Q3 volume decline, 8.8% YTD decline; US beer industry down 4-6% ([BeyondSPX](https://beyondspx.com/quote/TAP/)) | New CEO Rahul Goyal (Oct 2025), 400-position cut (~9% Americas salaried), $35-50M restructuring charges; focus on "core + economy + above-premium" | In progress -- too early to assess | -- | Stock down significantly from 2023 highs |
| Boston Beer (SAM) | 2021-2023 | Seltzer boom-bust: overproduced Truly hard seltzer, took $102M hard seltzer write-down in 2021 | SKU rationalization, innovation pivot to Twisted Tea (became #1 FMB), cost discipline | Partial recovery -- Twisted Tea success offset seltzer collapse | ~2 years | Recovered from ~$300 to ~$350 (partial from $1,300 peak) |
| Anheuser-Busch InBev | 2017-2020 | Post-SABMiller acquisition: $100B+ debt, volume declines in US, Bud Light market share erosion | Aggressive deleveraging ($15B+ debt reduction), portfolio premiumization (Michelob Ultra), cost-cutting | Partial -- deleveraged but US market share continued declining | 3+ years | Slow grind from ~$80 to ~$60 range |

**Pattern:** Brewer turnarounds typically require 2-3 years minimum. Success correlates with (1) portfolio innovation that catches a consumer trend (Twisted Tea, Michelob Ultra), (2) decisive cost restructuring, and (3) balance sheet deleveraging. Pure cost-cutting without brand revitalization tends to produce only temporary margin improvement while volumes continue eroding.

**3 of 3 distress cases show partial or incomplete recovery -> ~33% full recovery base rate for fundamental brewer distress. Analyst default: 50% band (low precedent confidence).**

## Risk Profile

- **Default risk type:** Cyclical / Structural
- **Cyclicality:** Low-to-Moderate (beer is relatively recession-resistant but facing secular decline)
- **Regulatory discretion:** Moderate (TTB/state liquor boards have meaningful discretion)
- **Macro sensitivity:**
  - Consumer confidence: Moderate (trade-down within category, not out of it)
  - Inflation: Moderate (input costs -- barley, aluminum, packaging -- pressure margins)
  - Interest rates: Low-Moderate (affects debt servicing for leveraged acquirers)
  - Trade policy/tariffs: Moderate (aluminum tariffs, import competition)
- **Binary trigger frequency:** Rare (category rarely produces overnight collapses)

### Kill Factors (hard reject -- maps to enrichment override triggers)

1. **Volume decline exceeding 8% annually for 2+ years without credible innovation pipeline** -- signals irreversible brand erosion. Example: generic regional brewers losing to craft/imports with no premium portfolio.
2. **Leverage above 5x net debt/EBITDA with declining EBITDA** -- combination of debt burden and shrinking cash flows creates liquidity spiral. Example: post-M&A brewers that overpaid for acquisitions.
3. **Loss of major distribution agreements** -- in three-tier system, losing distributor relationships is potentially fatal to volume recovery.

### Friction Factors (PCS modifiers -- reduce confidence, don't kill thesis)

1. **GLP-1 drug adoption uncertainty** -- suggested friction: -1, rationale: unknown magnitude of consumption impact, no historical precedent for this demand disruptor
2. **Secular beer-to-spirits/RTD shift** -- suggested friction: -1, rationale: structural category headwind reduces confidence in volume stabilization
3. **New CEO with untested turnaround plan** -- suggested friction: -1, rationale: Molson Coors' Goyal (Oct 2025) has no track record at TAP yet

## Thesis-Breaking Patterns

- **Death by a thousand cuts (volume erosion):** gradual -- beer losing share to spirits, RTD, NA alternatives, cannabis in legalized states. Examples: Molson Coors persistent US volume decline since 2016.
- **M&A overpayment trap:** binary-ish -- large acquisition at peak multiples followed by impairment. Examples: AB InBev/SABMiller ($100B+ debt), Molson Coors $3.65B impairment.
- **Consumer trend miss:** gradual then sudden -- missing a category shift (hard seltzer boom/bust). Examples: Boston Beer seltzer inventory write-down 2021.

## Evidence Requirements

### Must-Have Data Points

| Data Point | Where to Find It | Why It Matters |
|-----------|-----------------|----------------|
| Volume trends (barrels shipped) | 10-K Item 7 (MD&A); FMP revenue + price/mix decomposition | Volume is destiny in brewing -- revenue growth via price alone is unsustainable |
| Gross margin trend (3-5 year) | FMP income statement; 10-K Item 7 | Signals pricing power vs input cost absorption |
| Net debt / EBITDA | FMP key metrics; 10-K Item 8 | Leverage determines survival runway |
| Market share data | Nielsen/IRI data (cited in 10-K); Brewers Association annual reports | Competitive position trend |
| Brand portfolio mix (premium vs economy %) | 10-K segment reporting; earnings call transcripts | Premium mix determines future margin trajectory |
| CapEx vs D&A ratio | FMP cash flow statement | Under-investing (CapEx < D&A) signals milking, not turnaround |
| Distribution network changes | 10-K Item 1 (Business); earnings calls | Route-to-market health |
| Innovation pipeline / new SKUs | Earnings calls; trade press (Brewers Association, Beer Business Daily) | Leading indicator of future volume trends |

### Red Flags If Missing

- If volume data is not decomposed into price/mix vs organic volume, analyst cannot distinguish real growth from inflation pass-through
- If management does not disclose market share trends, assume they are losing share

## Epistemic Profile

| PCS Question | Typical Answer | Rationale |
|-------------|---------------|-----------|
| Q1: Operational risk? | Mostly Yes | Risk is primarily operational (volume, margins, portfolio management) but structural category decline adds uncertainty |
| Q2: Regulatory minimal? | Mostly Yes | TTB regulation is predictable; state-level liquor boards are stable. No major discretionary regulatory overhang |
| Q3: Historical precedent? | Partial | Some brewer turnarounds exist but success rate is low (~33%); secular volume decline is relatively new challenge |
| Q4: Non-binary outcome? | Yes | Gradient outcomes -- partial recovery, slow decline, margin stabilization without volume growth are all possible |
| Q5: Macro/geo limited? | Mostly Yes | Primarily domestic, limited FX exposure for US-focused brewers. Tariffs on inputs are a moderate factor |

**Epistemic stability:** 3/5 -- Brewer turnarounds have limited precedent in the current secular decline environment. GLP-1 drugs and RTD competition introduce novel uncertainties without historical analogs.
**Suggested PCS friction:** -1 -- Secular category headwind (beer volume decline) reduces predictability of any turnaround thesis.
